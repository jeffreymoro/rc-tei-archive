641. Robert Southey to John Rickman, 19 December 1801

(1)
MS: Huntington Library, RS 18Previously published: Kenneth Curry (ed.), New Letters of Robert Southey, 2 vols (London and New York, 1965), I, pp. 264-265.
Saturday. 19 Dec. 1801.

Yesterday your note arrived. this morning I have executed your commission – tho had not Xmas been so close at hand I should probably have presented a remonstrance against the wretched calvinistical cant of Hannah More – & recommended any thing instead – any chance number in Mr Lanes

(1)
William Lane (1745/6-1814; DNB), publisher of light literature and promoter of circulating libraries.
catalogue of novels in preference.

When I called with Allyballycarrickoshauglin

(2)
The bog which Castle Rackrent looks out over in Maria Edgeworth (1768-1849; DNB), Castle Rackrent (1800). Southey was presumably calling in on Rickman to leave the copies of Castle Rackrent that Rickman had ordered as gifts; see Rickman to Robert Southey, 5 December 1801, in Orlo Williams, Lamb’s Friend the Census Taker. Life and Letters of John Rickman (Boston and New York, 1912), pp. 67-68.
at the Cockpit
(3)
Government offices opposite Whitehall, used by the Privy Council office, where Rickman was directing work on the 1801 census.
I immediately discovered the Goul.
(4)
‘The Goul’ was a nickname for a Mr Simonds (or Simmonds), who worked for Rickman on the 1801 Census; see Rickman to Robert Southey, 7 November 1801, in Orlo Williams, Lamb’s Friend the Census Taker. Life and Letters of John Rickman (Boston and New York, 1912), p. 58.
I wish his picture were before me – it would call up uglier phantoms than Fuseli
(5)
Henry Fuseli (1741-1825; DNB), Swiss-born artist, whose work includes ‘The Nightmare’ (1781). Fuseli was rumoured to eat raw pork before sleeping in order to stimulate nightmares.
can create xx by digesting raw pork.

My comforts are sadly lessened here. my Mother is very ill – she will hardly live out the winter. so much of our time & attention is necessarily taken up in a sick room – that little is left for any thing else. – xxxx xxxxxx I continue unemployed – except in attending Walkers Lectures.

(6)
Probably given by Adam Walker (1730/1-1821; DNB), famed for his lectures, especially on astronomy.
in all probability – from being no secretary at all I shall soon be transmuted into a sort of semi-tutor. Corry is familiar & friendly. but it is certainly odd that he has never asked me to dinner.

farewell. yrs
R S.
