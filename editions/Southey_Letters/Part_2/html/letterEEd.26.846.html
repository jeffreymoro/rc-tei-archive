846. Robert Southey to Charles Watkin Williams Wynn, 28 October 1803

(1)
Address: To/ C W Williams Wynn Esqr. M.P./ Wynnstay/ WrexhamStamped: KESWICK/ 298Endorsement: 20 Oct <28th > 1803 MS: National Library of Wales, MS 4811DPreviously published: John Wood Warter (ed.), Selections from the Letters of Robert Southey, 4 vols (London, 1856), I, pp. 242-244.
Dear Wynn

If a Lieutenant Colonel who has all the volunteers of two huge countries command,

(1)
Wynn was in command of the Montgomeryshire Volunteers.
can find leisure for those researches which entitle him to the degree of F.A.S.
(2)
Possibly a jokey tribute to Wynn’s contributions to Southey’s researches on antique Welsh customs and history; rearranging the FSA of Fellow of the Society of Antiquaries.
he may help out a poem
(3)
Madoc (1805).
which certainly ought to entitle me to the Poet Laureatship of the Principality.

What was the dress of the Welsh? I have given Ririd

(4)
Madoc (1805), Part 1, Book 12, lines 116-122.
at a venture a shirt of fine linen – a tunic – an embroidered girdle – a mantle bordered with fur – & a fur cap – & he looks very well in it. Supposing that they had assimilated to Saxon decency I would have given him breeches, but neither breeches, small clothes, indescribables, pantaloons, nor galligaskins could be put in in English <verse>. Stockings may have been in use then, but could not when the King has a Pedifer to chaf his feet as he sate at table.
(5)
Madoc (1805), Part 1, Book 2, lines 95-96 and Note.

I am going to carry Madoc to Bardsey.

(6)
Madoc (1805), Part 1, Book 13.
if you have Powell
(7)
David Powell (1549/1552-1598; DNB), The Historie of Cambria, Now Called Wales (1584).
or Warrington
(8)
William Warrington (1735-1827), The History of Wales (1788), no. 2981 in the sale catalogue of Southey’s library.
at hand do tell me which of the old Kings were buried there. Owen Gwynedh
(9)
Owen Gwynedd (1100-1170, Prince of Gwynedd 1137-1170; DNB). The father of Madoc, in legend.
& his father Gryffedh
(10)
Gruffudd ap Cynan (1054/5-1137, Prince of Gwynedd 1081-1137; DNB).
were buried at Bangor. I could make a swelling & sonorous passage about the old gentlemen & their worthinesses – if I knew them. The extract which I made at Wynnstay from the Royal Tribes
(11)
Philip Yorke (1743-1804; DNB), The Royal Tribes of Wales (1799). Southey eventually acquired an edition, no. 3133 in the sale catalogue of his library.
& the Gwydir History
(12)
Sir John Wynn (1553-1627; DNB), The History of the Gwedir Family (1770). Southey eventually acquired an edition of 1827, no. 3133 in the sale catalogue of his library.
are become very useful. twas unfortunate that we did not visit Bardsey – I feel it now. this Welsh part of the poem
(13)
Southey goes on to describe some of the main events in Madoc (1805), Part 1, Books 10-15.
will be very Odyssey-like. I am weaving into it all the collectable circumstances of the time & manners of the peoples in this order. Journey to Mathrafal – the Hirlas Horn – the Grave of Jorwerth at Pennant Melangel the Meeting of the Bards. Dinevor & the Embassy of Gwgan of Caer Einion from the Royal Tribes.
(14)
Philip Yorke, The Royal Tribes of Wales (Wrexham, 1799), p. 55.
thus far is done. then come Bardsey & Llewelyn. the child of Hoel. the Excommunication of Owen Cyveilioc
(15)
Owain Cyfeiliog (c. 1125-1197, Prince of Powys 1160-1195; DNB).
at Bangor for not crusading – & the Priest detected by Madoc in digging a hole from his fathers grave thro into the church yard to eject his body, he having died under the censure of the church (from Giraldus
(16)
Giraldus Cambrensis (c. 1146- c. 1223; DNB), Itinerarium Cambriae (1191).
& your friend Mr Yorke.
(17)
Philip Yorke, The Royal Tribes of Wales (Wrexham, 1799), pp. 4-5.
) this will tell well & Madoc shall carry over the bones of Owen to America. I shall then try my strength with Camoens
(18)
Luis Vaz de Camoes (1524-1580), Os Lusiadas (1572)
& Valerius Flaccus
(19)
Gaius Valerius Flaccus (d. c. AD 90), Argonautica.
(who was a man of far more genius) – in the embarkation scene. I can find a place for only one picture – & that will be taken from the Llanberris scenery – about the village – not the Lake. Dinevor is such mere English scenery that I have but hinted at it to contrast it with glens & mountains. but the Towey had beavers in the days of Giraldus
(20)
Giraldus Cambrensis, Itinerarium Cambriae (1191).
& I have shown Madoc one poor hermit one to put him in mind of his own countrymen.
(21)
Madoc (1805), Part 1, Book 12, lines 35-43.
I wish your brother
(22)
Sir Watkin Williams Wynn (1772-1840).
would colonize the Dee with some of these old Welshmen. there is something to me very affecting in the extirpation of so interesting an animal.

Hei mihi

(23)
The Latin translates as ‘Oh dear’.
that I have written no song! whether it be that Madoc has monopolized my whole stock of ideas – or that my gift is in singing songs not writing them – My feelings when I have been trying are either the contempt that would make “vile ballads” about of mockery, or a forefeeling of triumph ready to break out into prophetic hymns of victory. I begin to fear they will not attempt invasion.

This war with Portugal

(24)
Britain and Portugal did not go to war and Portugal retained a precarious neutrality until 1807.
affects me in both senses of the word. of course it will drive my Uncle to England & so somewhat influence my choice of an abiding place. it cuts off all supply of books reducing me to feed upon the charity of great new libraries – for I have no resource but in Lord Bute
(25)
John Stuart, 1st Marquess of Bute (1744-1814; DNB), Ambassador to Spain 1795-1796 and a collector of books on Spain and Portugal.
– & it ruins the pleasantest hope I entertained – that of speedily crossing over to the land I love. God-a-mercy that a fellow
(26)
Napoleon Bonaparte (1769-1821, First Consul 1799-1804, Emperor of the French 1804-1814).
whelped in Corsica & living in France should interfere with the studies of a poor historian by the side of Lake Derwentwater!

God bless you. I am well & active both in body & mind – but hæret lateri!

(27)
Publius Vergilius Maro (70-19 BC), Aeneid, Book 4, line 73: ‘[it] clings to my side’, in the sense of an arrow in a deer.
yet I am the better for it – it seems to have connected me with the other world – given me new relations to it & loosened my roots here.

RS.

Friday night. 28 Oct. 1803.
