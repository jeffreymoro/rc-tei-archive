Places

This page gives further information about the key places that Southey lived in, visited or was connected to during the period 1798-1803.


  • Balliol: Southey was a student at Balliol College, Oxford in 1792-1794, though he left without taking a degree.

  • 25 Bridge St, Westminster: the London residence of Southey and his family from c. November 1801- late January 1802. His mother died there on 5 January 1802.

  • Brixton, a village just outside London: the country home of the Bedford family, Southey spent several weeks there in September–October 1793, during which time he wrote the first draft of Joan of Arc.

  • Burton: village in Hampshire, where Southey stayed in June–September 1797. He returned in October–November 1799 and rented a house found for him by his friend, Charles Biddlecombe.

  • Christ Church: town, just along the coast from Burton, Hampshire. Southey stayed there briefly in June 1797 and again in October 1799, while supervising renovations to the house he was renting at Burton.

  • Cintra [Sintra]: town in Portugal, where Southey’s uncle, Herbert Hill, kept a summer home. Southey visited the house in June–October 1800, calling it ‘a spot the most delightful I have ever yet known’.

  • College Green, Bristol: the home of Southey’s aunt, Elizabeth Tyler. He spent several years of his childhood living there and often returned in 1792-4. In October 1794 Miss Tyler threw Southey out of the house when she learned of his relationship with Edith Fricker and involvement in Pantisocracy.

  • Congreve, Staffordshire: the home of Mary Barker. Southey and his wife stopped there on their way to Keswick in August 1803, a journey precipitated by the death of their only child Margaret.

  • Cottles: Joseph Cottle’s bookshop in Bristol. This was at 48 High St from April 1791 to March 1798. Cottle then moved to 5 Wine St until financial troubles forced him to close his business in July 1799.

  • Crickhowel: small town in Powys. Southey visited it in October 1798 on his walking tour of South Wales with Charles Danvers.

  • Danvers’s, Kingsdown, Bristol: the home of Southey’s old friend Charles Danvers and his mother. Used by Southey as postal address on his return to Bristol from Portugal in 1801.

  • Dublin: Southey visited the Irish capital for about 10 days in October 1801 at the beginning of his employment as secretary to Isaac Corry.

  • Duke St, London: the city home of Isaac Corry, Southey’s employer in 1801–1802.

  • Gray’s Inn: Southey was seeking to qualify as a barrister between 1797 and 1801. This required him to register at one of the Inns of Court and occasionally attend formal dinners there. Southey chose Grays Inn, at the intersection of High Holborn and Grays Inn Road.

  • Greta Hall/ Greeta Hall: House on the outskirts of Keswick. From 1800, the home of Samuel Taylor Coleridge and his family. Southey and his wife visited in August 1801 and made it their permanent residence from September 1803.

  • Keswick: market town in the Lake District. From 1800, the home of Samuel Taylor Coleridge and his family. Southey and his wife visited in August 1801 and made it their permanent residence from September 1803.

  • Kingsdown Parade, Bristol: Southey lived there December 1799–February 1800, close to Charles Danvers.

  • 12 Lambs Conduit Street: Southey’s lodgings in London, December 1797–February 1798.

  • Lanthony: ruined priory in Powys. Southey visited it in October 1798 on his walking tour of South Wales with Charles Danvers. The estate was later owned by Walter Savage Landor.

  • Maes Gywn, Neath: house in the Vale of Neath, near Swansea, that Southey attempted – but failed – to rent in autumn 1802.

  • Martin Hall: Southey’s name for the house in Westbury, near Bristol, which he rented June 1798–June 1799.

  • Merthyr Tidvil: home of George Maber, who was the local Rector. Southey visited him on October 1798 on his walking tour of South Wales with Charles Danvers.

  • Norwich: Southey visited William Taylor at his home at Surry St in Norwich May-June 1798.

  • 12 St James’s Place, Kingsdown, Bristol: the house, near to Charles Danvers, rented by Southey and his wife from May 1802–August 1803. Their first child, Margaret, was born and died there. Her death prompted them to leave Bristol for good. In future years, Southey only returned on occasional visits.

  • Stokes Croft: area of Bristol, north of the city centre. Southey briefly lived there March–April 1800.

  • Stowey: the village of Nether Stowey in Somerset. Home of Tom Poole, who found a cottage there for the Coleridge family between 1797 and 1799. Southey visited in August 1799, after his reconciliation with Coleridge.

  • 35 Strand, London: the home of Southey and his family from Southey from late January until late May 1802.

  • Westbury: village just outside Bristol, Southey rented a cottage, which he named Martin-Hall, between June 1798 and June 1799.

  • 8 Westgate Buildings, Bath: the boarding house run by Margaret Southey from 1793 to 1798.

  • Wynnstay, near Ruabon, Denbighshire: the home of the Wynn family.

  • Yarmouth: town on the Norfolk coast; Southey visited his brother Henry Herbert Southey, who was studying with George Burnett, there in late May 1798.
