298. Robert Southey to Charles Watkin Williams Wynn, [25 March] 1798

(1)
Address: To/ C W Williams Wynn Esqr/ 5. Stone Buildings/ Lincolns Inn/ LondonPostmarks: FREE MR/ 26/ 98; B/ MR/ 98Endorsement: March 25 Lady day 1798MS: National Library of Wales, MS 4811DUnpublished.
Lady day. 98
My dear Wynn

I thank you for your list of books. they shall come in succession after I have digested Coke,

(1)
Edward Coke (1552–1643; DNB), Institutes of the Laws of England (1628–1644).
a tough dish, & not the more palatable for the notes that season it. I am however dieting upon it to some effect.

To what you say of marriage I reply not — because my own opinion inclines to yours, & the habit of arguing pro & con, tho perhaps it may be well for a lawyer, is calculated to produce a scepticism of mind, an unsettled state of mind, prejudicial to virtue & happiness.

Our Garden scheme

(2)
John May and Southey’s plan for a convalescent hospital.
is, I hope, advancing by this time it is probable that John May has seen you, & told you what Martin
(3)
Probably Matthew Martin (1748–1838; DNB), secretary to the Society for Bettering the Condition and Improving the Comforts of the Poor.
suggested. recollection made me smile when I wrote his name.

My book

(4)
Joan of Arc (1798).
will soon be finished. I will send it you as soon as possible, & also, if you think it will be right, a copy for Richards.
(5)
Probably Sir Richard Richards (1752–1823; DNB), an eminent lawyer in Chancery.
it will be civil as the large copies are not for sale, & it will be very handsome.
(6)
Cottle printed larger copies of Southey’s works, to be distributed as gifts; see Robert Southey to Joseph Cottle [c. 18 May 1797], The Collected Letters of Robert Southey. Part 1, Letter 217.

I heard of Martin Butt last week. he is a curate, & near the parsonage house on which his father wasted so much money.

(7)
George Butt (1741–1795; DNB), the father of Southey’s schoolmate John Marten Butt, had incurred considerable expense building a new parsonage at Stanford, Worcestershire, where he was Rector.
I shall probably see him in the summer, & pass some days in that neighbourhood.

I see that Lewis has castrated his Monk, greatly to his improvement.

(8)
Matthew Gregory Lewis (1775–1818; DNB) published a fourth, expurgated edition of his controversial novel in 1798, retitling it Ambrosio, or, The Monk.
it was a good plan to make his book popular first, & decent afterwards. I think I have added a good motto to my second edition, on the back of the title page. it is from Erasmus. Ut homines, ita libros, indies seipsis meliores fieri oportet.
(9)
The Latin translates as ‘Books are like people: they ought to improve on themselves day by day.’ It was used as an epigraph to Joan of Arc (1798) and is a quotation from the works of Desidirius Erasmus (c. 1476–1536), Dutch scholar.

You will find a fine anecdote in my notes of the Irish, which will amuse you. a custom of leaving the right arm unchristened, that they might give a more deadly & ungracious blow with it — & then you know the Christian part had not to answer for the sin.

(10)
Joan of Arc, 2 vols (Bristol, 1798), I, p. 153.

Who was it used to thank God for being born a man not a beast — a Greek not a barbarian?

(11)
A saying attributed to various Greek philosophers, including Thales (624–546 BC), Socrates (469–399 BC) and Plato (428–348 BC).
as a Hottentot I might have been very well contented — but indeed I am thankful I was not made an Irishman.

God bless you. yrs affectionately
R Southey.
