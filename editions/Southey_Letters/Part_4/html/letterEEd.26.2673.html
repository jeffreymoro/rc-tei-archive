2673. Robert Southey to Grosvenor Charles Bedford, 6 December 1815

(1)
Address: To/ G. C. Bedford Esqre/ Exchequer/ WestminsterStamped: KESWICK/ 298Postmark: E/ 9 DEC 9/ 1815Endorsement: 6 Decr. 1815MS: Bodleian Library, MS Eng. Lett. c. 25Unpublished.
Wednesday 6 Dec. 1815
My dear Grosvenor

Here we are, safe & sound, & without accident, & we have found all well, – God be thanked. Our return was a sort of triumph, – & you would have been as well pleased at the children had you heard me harangue like a show man as we unpackd the play things & exhibited them one after the other.

My table is spread with letters, – by the next post I shall endeavour to draw up the memorial

(1)
A memorial to request that the import duty on the books Southey had bought on his recent travels be waived or reduced; see Southey to Charles Watkin Williams Wynn, 16 December 1815, Letter 2683.
& send to you thro Rickman.
(2)
The memorial was enclosed in Southey to John Rickman, 8 December 1815, Letter 2680.

I have a farther cause of quarrel in the Q Review.

(3)
Southey was unhappy with changes made by Croker to his review of Eustache-Auguste Carel (1788–1836), Précis Historique de la Guerre d’Espagne et de Portugal, de 1808 à 1814 (1815); Jean Sarrazin (1770–1848), Histoire de la Guerre d’Espagne et de Portugal, de 1807 à 1814 (1814); General View of the Political State of France, and of the Government of Louis XVIII (1815); An Answer to the Calumniators of Louis XVIII (1815); Official Accounts of the Battle of Waterloo (1815); Lieutenant-General W. A. Scott (dates unknown), Battle of Waterloo (1815), Quarterly Review, 13 (July 1815), 448–526. See So…
Look at p 476 & see what is said of the Convention of Cintra.
(4)
Under the terms of the Convention of Cintra (30 August 1808) the defeated French were allowed to evacuate their troops from Portugal.
My words were these (for luckily we preserved the sheets as originally printd) – ‘a transaction of which the pernicious moral effects would have outweighed its political advantage had these even been ten times greater than its most interested defenders could ever have th imagined it to possess. This disgraceful event came fearfully in aid of the despondents”
(5)
Southey objected that a section praising Wellington’s reaction to the Convention as ‘a presage of [his] … extraordinary military foresight’ had been interpolated; Quarterly Review, 13 (July 1815), 476.
&c – You may be sure I shall decry the impudent alteration which has been made. These people little know the man whom they have to deal with!

God bless you. Remember me to all at home, & tell your brother I shall never enjoy his society as I wish to do, until he makes a holy day & comes down to spend it here.

RS.

I must beg to remedy a neglect xxxxxxxx occasioned by a forgetfulness of always carrying silver about me. Give Knox

(6)
John William Knox (1784–1862), clergyman, scholar and usher at Westminster School 1806–1821.
10/6, & request him to give it for me, or more properly for Edith to Mrs Vardons servant Mary.
(7)
Mary’s surname and dates are unknown.
The black Night
(8)
i.e. Atra Nox, a nickname for Knox.
will have opportunities of executing this commission.
