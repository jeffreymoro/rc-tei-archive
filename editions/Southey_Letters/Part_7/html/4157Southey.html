4157. Robert Southey to Caroline Bowles, 18 March 1824

(1)
Endorsement: No 49. To Miss Caroline Bowles MS: British Library, Add MS 47889. ALS; 2p. Unpublished.
Keswick18 March 1824

Your letter was a relief to me, – for I was afraid illness had been the cause of your silence, – & it brought news of your amendment. – These hasty lines accompany a few more stanzas

(1)
Southey was beginning ‘Robin Hood’. The resulting incomplete work was published in Robin Hood: a Fragment. By the Late Robert Southey, and Caroline Southey. With Other Fragments and Poems By R.S. and C.S. (London, 1847), pp. [1]–36.
– When next you write, speak of the poem on a slip of paper by itself, & let your letter have no allusion to it. I expect much amusement from keeping the secret, & some advantage from it. What I write generally receives a good deal of amendment before it passes thro the press, – wherever you can mend a passage, do so. I shall soon finish the canto. – Do you describe how the infant <boy> like a pet lamb that had lost its mother – was fondled & spoilt.
(2)
Caroline Bowles did not include these lines in ‘Robin Hood’.

What I said about two ounces

(3)
Southey to Caroline Bowles, 13 February 1824, Letter 4133. Southey and Bowles were corresponding through John Rickman, and thus making use of his privilege, as a senior officer of Parliament, to transmit post free of charge. However, this privilege only applied to letters of less than two ounces’ weight.
