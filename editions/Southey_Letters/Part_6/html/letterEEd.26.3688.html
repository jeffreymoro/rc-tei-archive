3688. Robert Southey to Grosvenor Charles Bedford, 27 May 1821

Address: To/ G. C. Bedford Esqre/ Exchequer
Endorsements: 27 May 1821; 27 May 1821
MS: Bodleian Library, MS Eng. Lett. c. 26. ALS; 4p.
Unpublished.

My dear Grosvenor

First I must address you as my Chancellor of the Exchequer & First Lord, requesting that you will settle all demands for my Stewardship at the Westminster Meeting, – an office which I should not have accepted had it not been my Uncles wish that I should so do.

(1)
An annual meeting in London of ex-pupils of Westminster School; Southey had agreed to share the post of Steward and thus take responsibility for some of the costs.
– My next request is that when you may be near King Street, Covent Garden, you will call at No 24 & pay £3.10/ to Joseph Lock the shoemaker,
(2)
Unidentified beyond the information given here.
who was born in the same street with me, & is as perfect in his way as poor Hyde,
(3)
Hyde (d. 1820) was Southey’s London tailor.
or the living Cockbaine.
(4)
John Cockbaine (1786–1873), Keswick draper and tailor.
He is therefore worth your seeing, tell him that you are an intimate friend of mine, & set him going, & I think you will pronounce him worthy of being appointed Boot & Shoemaker to the Butler.
(5)
‘The Butler’ was a mythical hero of Southey’s and Bedford’s devising, about whom they had spun comical stories since their schooldays.

I think you have forgotten to order certain clothes for me from Hydes successor, of whom I could not order them myself, because I know not where he xxxx what his name is. Will you therefore, if it has not been already done, write a note to him, & desire him to send me a black coat & waistcoat, a pair of loose trousers, kerseymeer of any such mixture as may be “in vogue,” – & a pair of black loose trowsers, for dress, – this last article I add now to the former order, – but if you have already given the order it is not worth while to make the addition.

I sent Gifford yesterday the second part of an article for the QR. which will be finished I trust in the present week. It is a life of Oliver Cromwell.

(6)
Southey’s ‘Life of Cromwell’, Quarterly Review, 25 (July 1821), 279–347.
I hope he will not turn it over to a future number, – if he inserts it in this, he may reckon on one for the next upon Brazil & Portugal
(7)
This article was not published.

The 25th sheet of the Peninsular War

(8)
Southey’s History of the Peninsular War (1823–1832).
came by this days post. I shall be glad to hear that you have obtained the clean sheets – you will find more time & satisfaction in reading them piece meal thus, than in sitting down to a formidable work as a sort of task. – You will very soon receive the story of Aguirre xx Lope de Aguirre in a little volume per se.
(9)
Southey’s The Expedition of Orsua; and the Crimes of Aguirre (1821), originally intended to be part of the History of Brazil (1810–1819) and first published in Edinburgh Annual Register, for 1810, 3.2 (1812), i–l.

Cupn is well, & amuses the whole house with his dialect. I have a wicked cold. The weather is abominable, little above the freezing point by day, & below it at night. Prester John

(10)
A cat.
is not yet arrived. – I have bottled some strong beer which you are to bear a part in drinking, – but if you like Worcestershire perry better, you shall have it. And you shall have toasted cheese to eat with it; & an herb pudding.

God bless you
RS

27 May. 1821
