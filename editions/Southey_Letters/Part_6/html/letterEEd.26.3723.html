3723. Robert Southey to Charles Watkin Williams Wynn, 1 September 1821

(1)
MS: National Library of Wales, MS 4813D. ALS; 3p. Previously published: John Wood Warter (ed.), Selections from the Letters of Robert Southey, 4 vols (London, 1856), III, pp. 268–270.
My dear Wynn

Learning from you that Αναξ ανδρων

(1)
‘King of men’; i.e. Edward Combe.
is still in the land of the living, I have done what uncertainty upon that point, has long withheld me from doing, – written to him. Poor fellow were we ever to meet again (& yet I hope we shall) – the first feeling would bring tears into our eyes, – an hour or two of delightful reminiscences would follow, & perhaps when the old stock of sympathy was exhausted we should be at a loss for other topics, & perceive how widely we have grown asunder.

I do not remember Dickins.

(2)
Henry John Dickens (c. 1771–1830), Captain of Westminster School in 1787. He had probably left the school before Southey arrived in 1788. In later life Dickens was a barrister.
Wintle
(3)
Robert Wintle (d. 1848), an old Westminster schoolfellow of Wynn and Southey. He was Rector of Culham 1797–1848.
I should have guessed to be as little susceptible of change as the Saracens Head.
(4)
A common crest in heraldry and a popular inn sign.
But I should have thought the Bridegroom DOyly
(5)
Thomas D’Oyly married Elizabeth Simons (dates unknown), daughter of Nicholas Simons (1754–1839), Rector of St Margaret’s, Canterbury 1807–1822, Rector of Ickham 1822–1839, on 4 January 1820, Morning Chronicle, 6 January 1820.
as little to be aged, a Badger you know is always grey – & he was an old fellow at school. – Barns claimed acquaintance with me at Oxford last year,
(6)
When Southey received his honorary Doctorate of Civil Law on 14 June 1820.
& I certainly should not have known him, tho when he named himself I recognized something of his features.

Did you ever see a sword of Cromwells

(7)
Oliver Cromwell (1599–1658; Lord Protector 1653–1658; DNB).
age with <having> the back of the blade hollow, & the hilt loaded with quicksilver, so as to give weight to the blow?
(8)
Southey had been led to this train of thought by an unusual old sword he had seen at Netherhall, home of his friend Humphrey Senhouse.
– I forget where I have seen an allusion to such a weapon
(9)
Henry More (1614–1687; DNB), ‘Preface’, to ‘A Platonick Song of the Soul’ in Philosophicall Poems (1647), no. 1998 in the sale catalogue of Southey’s library. Southey noted the passage in Common-Place Book, ed. John Wood Warter, 4 series (London, 1849–1850), II, p. 6.
– either in Fuller, I think, or in Jeremy Taylor.
(10)
The works of Thomas Fuller (1608–1661; DNB) or Jeremy Taylor (1613–1667; DNB); like More they were seventeenth-century clergymen.
A clumsy contrivance, & more likely to make the owner of the sword wield it awkwardly, than to give him any advantage. – The mention of J Taylor reminds me of some gross misprints in Reginald Hebers edition,
(11)
Reginald Heber, The Whole Works of the Right Rev. Jeremy Taylor, D. D. (1822), no. 2782 in the sale catalogue of Southey’s library.
which I wish I had marked at the time that he might have given orders to cancel the leaves. A careful Corrector of the press is hardly now to be found in any printing office. The octavo edition of Burkes works, which I possess
(12)
The Works of the Right Honourable Edmund Burke (1815–1827), no. 492 in the sale catalogue of Southey’s library; an edition of the writings of Edmund Burke (1729–1797; DNB), conservative political theorist, philosopher and politician.
is made in many places utterly unintelligible by its extreme inaccuracy.

Have you seen the most laborious work that ever proceeded from Wales? Major Prices Retrospect of Mahommedan History

(13)
David Price (1762–1835; DNB), Chronological Retrospect, or, Memoirs of the Principal Events of Mohammedan History (1811–1821), no. 2350 in the sale catalogue of Southey’s library.
– from Oriental authorities in four quarto volumes, printed in Brecknock? From the last volume I learn, what I could have forewarned him of (had I known him) before he sent the first to the Press, – that there are not many persons like myself, who will purchase such a work.

I am going to Lowther in the course of this month, & wish you were to be there. You shall have a portion of O Newman

(14)
Southey’s unfinished epic, ‘Oliver Newman’, set in New England. A fragment was published posthumously in Oliver Newman: a New-England Tale (Unfinished): with Other Poetical Remains by the Late Robert Southey (London, 1845), pp. 1–90.
in two or three days

God bless you
RS.

1 Sept. 1821
