3309. Robert Southey to John Murray, 4 June 1819

Address: To/ John Murray Esqre./ Albemarle Street/ London
Stamped: KESWICK/ 298
Postmark: E/ 7 JU 7/ 1819
Endorsement: 1819 July 19/ Southey, R.
MS: National Library of Scotland, MS 42552. ALS; 3p.
Unpublished.

Keswick. 4 June. 1819.
My dear Sir

Thank you for your note & draft. – I have a good many materials for the Times of Charles 1 & 2d

(1)
Charles I (1600–1649; King of Great Britain 1625–1649; DNB) and Charles II (1630–1685; King of Great Britain 1660–1685; DNB). Southey was considering writing on this topic for the Quarterly Review.
– for the sake of which indeed I had retained Evelyn.
(2)
Southey’s review of Memoirs, Illustrative of the Life and Writings of John Evelyn (1818) had appeared in Quarterly Review, 19 (April 1818), 1–54.
Indeed I have for so many years <been> in the habit of marking whatever relates to the state of manners & opinions, & the transitions of society, that I have frequently thought I could produce a view of the Moral History of England (using the word moral in its wide sense) which would be as original in its conception, as it would be curious.
(3)
This project did not result in a book; the surviving notes that Southey put together were posthumously published as ‘Collections for the History of Manners and Literature in England’, Common-Place Book, ed. John Wood Warter, 4 series (London, 1849–1850), I, pp. 439–578.
– By all means send me the third volume of Marlborough,
(4)
The third volume of William Coxe, Memoirs of John Duke of Marlborough, with his Original Correspondence; Collected from the Family Records at Blenheim, and Other Authentic Sources. Illustrated with Portraits, Maps, and Military Plans (1818–1819). Southey’s account of Coxe’s study appeared in Quarterly Review, 23 (May 1820), 1–73.
& with it the account of her own conduct which his Dutchess published,
(5)
Sarah Churchill, Duchess of Marlborough (1660–1744; DNB), An Account of the Conduct of the Dowager Duchess of Marlborough (1742).
– & Somervilles Hist: of Queen Anne:
(6)
Thomas Somerville (1741–1830; DNB), The History of Great Britain During the Reign of Queen Anne (1798).
– I believe I have all the other historical works which could be useful. – Cowpers life is already well known; – he stands where he deserves to stand in the public opinion, & I am always desirous of chusing those subjects where the materials are of value, whatever may be the workmanship.
(7)
Murray had proposed that Southey write on the poet William Cowper (1731–1800; DNB). Although Southey declined the idea here, he was to go on to edit a fifteen-volume edition of The Works of William Cowper, Esq. Comprising his Poems, Correspondence, and Translations. With a Life of the Author (1835–1837).
I shall have an interesting paper for you upon the Monastic Orders,
(8)
Southey’s review of Thomas Fosbrooke (1770–1842; DNB), British Monachism; or, Manners and Customs of the Monks and Nuns of England (1817), Quarterly Review, 22 (July 1819), 59–102.
connected with the Establishment at Braybrook House under Lady Eleanor King, in which the Queen took so much interest.
(9)
Lady Isabella Lettice King (1772–1845; DNB) founded the Ladies’ Association at Bailbrook House, near Bath, in June 1816. It provided a home for orphaned gentlewomen with no income and was duly praised by Southey in his article in Quarterly Review, 22 (July 1819), 96–101. Queen Charlotte (1744–1818; DNB) had been a supporter and had visited Bailbrook on 3 December 1817.

I am on the very point of compleating my great labour, – much to my joy in having accomplishd it, & much to my satisfaction when I look upon the quantity of information which never was laid before the public in any other shape – it is a huge volume – not less than 850 pages.

(10)
Southey was finishing the final volume of his History of Brazil (1810–1819). It ran to 950 pages.
You will probably see me about the end of the month, – the first object of my journey is to get at certain papers which I have <been> invited to inspect, concerning the Peninsular War, – & as soon as I return we go to press with it, vigorously.
(11)
Southey had been invited to see papers in the possession of Sir Henry Bunbury and Edward Moor, relating to his History of the Peninsular War (1823–1832).

Believe me my dear Sir Yrs very truly
Robert Southey
