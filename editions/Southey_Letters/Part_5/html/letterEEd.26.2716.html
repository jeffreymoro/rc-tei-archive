2716. Robert Southey to John Rickman, 16 February 1816

(1)
Address: To/ John Rickman Esqre/ St Stephens Court/ New Palace Yard/ Westminster Stamped: KESWICK/ 298 Postmark: FREE/ 19 FE 19/ 1816 Endorsement: 16. Febry 1816 MS: Huntington Library, RS 266. ALS; 2p. Unpublished.
My dear R.

I never like my own lucubrations so well as when they first come to me in good legible typography. I suspect however that by the world in general there is a sort of pleasure attached to seeing things in manuscript somewhat like that derived from eating bread in secret; – so I send the {inclosed} open in its way, that if there be any gratification of this kind, Mrs R. may enjoy it.

(1)
The manuscript of part of Southey’s poem The Poet’s Pilgrimage to Waterloo (1816).
When she has done with it, transmit it to my Factotum Bedford, who gratifies himself while he is doing any business for me, & who has not so much business of his own but that I may without scruple create a little for him.

God bless you
RS.

More tomorrow. I fear we shall have to wait for the engravers.

(2)
The Poet’s Pilgrimage to Waterloo (1816) featured engravings made from seven sketches drawn on the spot by Southey’s friend Edward Nash, and one by Charles Bell (1774–1842; DNB).

(4)
The simurgh is a fabulous bird in Iranian mythology. It appeared prominently in Southey’s Thalaba the Destroyer (1801). Prince Leopold was a ‘Cock-Simorg’ because, like the bird, he was expected to bestow fertility on the land – in his case by providing Princess Charlotte with an heir to the throne.

(5)
The Lay of the Laureate. Carmen Nuptiale (1816), ‘The Dream’, stanzas 19–21. Comparison with the earlier draft of the poem in Keswick Museum and Art Gallery, Box B/414, reveals Southey simply changed the description of the lion from ‘Belgic’, representing Prince William, to ‘Saxon’, representing Prince Leopold, as both the royal houses of the Netherlands and Saxe-Coburg featured lions as supporters on their coats of arms.

(6)
Stephen Polito (1763/4–1814) was the owner of a menagerie and exhibited beasts at Bartholomew Fair and Exeter Change in London.
