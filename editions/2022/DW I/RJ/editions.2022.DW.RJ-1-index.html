Index of Key People and Places in Rydal Journals Notebook 1
(DCMS 104.1, 1824–1825)

This index provides basic details on individuals and sites referenced most regularly in the first Rydal Journals notebook, emphasizing the person or place’s importance to Dorothy Wordsworth in 1824–1825. Each individual’s age at the beginning of 1825 appears in parentheses after his or her name. More general bios for many included here are available in this edition’s Biographical Index, and, when available, links are provided for corresponding entries in Oxford’s online Dictionary of National Biography.



The Family at Rydal Mount

Wordsworth, Dorothy (53 years old, DNB): Active, healthy, and still in her intellectual and social prime (see headnote to Notebook 1).

Wordsworth, William (54, DNB): DW’s beloved brother and long-time housemate. By the mid-1820s, WW enjoyed a growing literary reputation and greater freedom to travel from home as his children came of age and the household had more disposable income. Besides frequent periods when intense eye pain rendered him unable to read or write, he was otherwise, by DW’s report, “well but much thinner than he used to be, and this makes him look old.”

(1)
Letters, 4:332.
Along with his responsibilities as Distributor of Stamps for Westmorland and political activities as a major supporter of Tory causes and opponent of Reform, he still regularly composed new poems and revised old ones, often with assistance from DW and MW.
(2)
See DW’s 28 May 1825 account of how she and MW were helping WW revise The Excursion, the book-length poem originally published in 1815 (Letters, 4:354).
The Wordsworths spent 1825 in dread of possibly losing the lease to Rydal Mount at year’s end, with WW musing, “It would almost break our hearts to be forced to quit it.”
(3)
Letters 4:370.

Wordsworth, Mary Hutchinson (54): WW’s wife and DW’s dear friend and housemate since 1802. Increasingly prone, like her husband, to wanderlust, MW spent much of the fall of 1824 and summer of 1825 visiting family and friends outside the Lake District. Such trips were largely possible because of DW’s willingness to stay at Rydal Mount supervising the household staff and any children left behind.

Wordsworth, John (21): WW and MW’s eldest child. DW reported in 1822 that John was “wonderfully improved in appearance during the last half-year,” as he had “cast off a portion of his rusticity” and “his limbs are knitting together – they do not look so large and clumsy as last year.” More flatteringly, she added, “He has a manly generous mind – gentle disposition and a serious love of study. Indeed he is a thoroughly excellent Youth.”

(4)
Letters, 4:105.
After struggling at Cambridge, John had transferred to Oxford in the fall of 1823. During much of the year covered by Notebook 1, however, he was home on leave from school.

Wordsworth, Dora (20): WW and MW’s second child and only surviving daughter. In 1821, DW described her then-17-year-old niece as “a sweet creature – no beauty now but interesting to look upon, though her health is by no means established.”

(5)
Letters, 4:89.
Although this fragile health would dictate much of her life until her early death at 42, Dora was generally lively and energetic in the mid-1820s. She spent part of 1823 living in Ambleside as a temporary teacher at the Dowlings’ school for girls and, after returning home, functioned increasingly as her father’s scribe and traveling companion. Her distant cousin Tom Robinson’s brief, and ill-fated, attempt to woo her in the autumn of 1825 is obliquely chronicled in Notebook 1.

Wordsworth, Willy (14): WW and MW’s youngest surviving child. After his two immediate elder siblings (Thomas and Catherine) died in 1812, Willy grew up surrounded by overprotective adults who generally opted to school him at home rather than expose him to germ-infested dormitories. In late 1823 DW reported that “Willy continues stout and well, and improves as much in scholarship as can be expected . . . of one so lively and volatile as he is.”

(6)
Letters, 4:239.
Sixteen months later, in May 1825, DW worried MW’s hovering would stifle the child, confiding, “Willy [is] strong and lively – but poor thing! his mother is often haunted by inward fears, and I cannot get rid of them.”
(7)
Letters, 4:345.

Hutchinson, Sara (49): MW’s unmarried younger sister who intermittently lived with the Wordsworths throughout adulthood. From February 1823 through August 1825, SH was away from Rydal, staying or traveling with friends and relatives in London, Boulogne, Devon, Herefordshire, and Wales. At the outset of Notebook 1, she was with her dying cousin, Thomas Monkhouse, in Wales and then, following his February 1825 death, his grieving family in Herefordshire.



Neighbors and Friends

Barber, Samuel (d. 1832): A wealthy old bachelor known for his eccentric outfits and tireless remodeling of Gell’s Cottage (now Silver How), the home west of Grasmere Lake he acquired from its namesake in 1808.

(8)
In early 1824, MW criticized Barber’s obsessive renovations as “too ridiculous for anything” (LMW, 107). An excerpt from Edward Quillinan’s vivid sketch of Barber’s eccentricity appears in Letters, 5:507–08. Woof provides details on Gell’s original construction of the cottage in GJ, 161–62.
Barber’s fortune apparently came from a mercery and drapery business in Wrexham, Wales, he inherited from his stepfather.
(9)
Notices of Barber’s partnerships with his stepfather, Charles Woollam, and then, following Woollam’s death, his mother appeared in the Chester Courant on 12 January 1796 and 26 December 1797.
While Barber’s radical leanings could rankle WW, the Wordsworth women and children considered him a beloved, if eccentric, uncle figure. In 1821, DW dubbed Barber “the gayest Bachelor you ever saw,” noting, “He calls at our door every day with the Morning Chronicle.”
(10)
Letters, 4:33.
By 1829 her outings with him had become so regular that the family teasingly referred to Barber as DW’s “Lover.”
(11)
Letters, 5:172.
After his unexpected death in 1832, Gell’s Cottage passed to his stepsister, Penelope Woollam Parry, and her husband, William, who put it up for sale within five years.
(12)
See advertisements for “The Cottage” in the Leeds Intelligencer, 13 May 1837; WG, 16 June 1838; and Liverpool Standard, 19 March 1839.

Barlow, Frances Bayley (40) and Fanny (17): A well-connected widow and her heiress daughter who joined the Wordsworths’ social set soon after occupying The Wood in Troutbeck in late 1824 or early 1825.

(13)
The 1 May 1825 entry in RJ includes the earliest mention of the Barlows in the Wordsworths’ papers. Various letters Dora wrote that summer show Fanny to be a new acquaintance (LDW, 22, 27). Sources placing the Barlows at The Wood ca. 1825–1830 include Letters, 5:178; Gordon, "Christopher North,” 297; and Baines, Companion to the Lakes, 286.
Raised in Manchester by genteel Presbyterians, Frances married John Barlow, heir to Middlethorpe Hall near York, in 1807. Upon his unexpected death in 1813, she and her only child, Fanny, were forced to vacate Middlethorpe. But by 1825 a series of subsequent deaths had unexpectedly left Fanny heir to this ancestral home, a fact to which SH alluded in describing Fanny as “a young heiress, who by John [Wordsworth]’s manner [SH suspected] he [had] some notion of.”
(14)
WT MS WLL / Hutchinson, Sara / 2 / 110a. Notice of the Barlows’ marriage appeared in several newspapers, including the Leeds Intelligencer for 12 January 1807. On 30 April 1813, the Stamford Mercury attributed John’s death to “an inflammation on his lungs, occasioned by neglecting a cold.” For the twists and turns of the Middlethorpe inheritance, see Worsley, “Middlethorpe Hall, Yorkshire.” 12 December 1985.
The Wordsworths and Barlows’ acquaintance proved fleeting, as Fanny married a Cheshire rector in 1828, and her mother a Scottish squire in 1834.
(15)
Fanny’s marriage to the Reverend E. Trafford Leigh was noticed in the Yorkshire Gazette for 22 November 1828. After Leigh’s passing, she remarried Eason Wilkinson in 1851 but died herself six months later (“The Late Dr. Eason Wilkinson,” Manchester Courier, 29 July 1878, 5). For Mrs. Barlow’s second marriage to Francis Hamilton of Lanarkshire, Scotland, see the Perthshire Courier for 11 December 1834.

Campbell, Col. Archibald (48) and Ann Blachford (39) : A retired veteran of the Peninsular Wars and his wife who had moved into Allan Bank (the Wordsworths’ former home in Grasmere) in 1820.

(16)
Letters, 4:225.
After learning that they would be moving to the Isle of Mull in late 1825, Dora wrote that the whole household would be “grieved to lose” these “excellent neighbours.”
(17)
WT MS WLL / Wordsworth, Dora / 1 / 2.
The families would continue corresponding on occasion until the colonel’s death in 1840.
(18)
See LMW, 228, and his death notice in the Gentleman’s Magazine, January 1841, 109.

Carr, Thomas (29) and Ann Dowling (40): Two associates of the Wordsworths, who, despite their 11-year age gap, had wed in February 1824 and settled in Ambleside at Cumpstone Lodge. After Richard Scambler’s unexpected death in 1820, Mr. Carr replaced him as the local apothecary and the Wordsworths’ family physician. He thereafter entered their social circle upon marrying the former Miss Dowling, Dora’s beloved ex-teacher at the Ambleside girls school she ran with her sisters. Still adjusting to their marriage, Sara Coleridge gossiped in April 1825 that “they are both estimable persons, when viewed separately, but seen as man & wife they are absurd enough.”

(19)
WT MS WLMS A / Coleridge, Sara / 9. In the same letter, Sara called Mr. Carr “a treasure” to the area, reporting that “the Wordsworths & other persons who have had experience of his good sense, & skill in his profession, have the highest opinion of both.” On Dora’s attachment to the former Miss Dowling, see Letters, 4:31 and 4:63.

Coleridge, Hartley (28, NB): The eldest son of Samuel Taylor and Sarah Coleridge. After a disastrous stint at Oxford, Hartley grew resigned to “not expecting much happiness” in life and accepted a teaching post at his old school in Ambleside. Having partly raised him, the Wordsworths feared “poor Hartley” was following his father’s lead in squandering remarkable talents.

(20)
Letters, 4:288, 4:340n.
In 1822, DW said Hartley resembled “a Portuguese Jew,” calling him “the oddest looking creature you ever saw – not taller than Mr de Quincey– with a beard as black as a raven.”
(21)
Letters, 4:169.
His sister Sara, however, reported in April 1825 that he “looks well, though he complains of being much fagged by the business of the school, not so much the teaching part as the keeping the boys in order, which he has not the art of doing, poor fellow!”
(22)
WT MS WLMS A / Coleridge, Sara / 9.

Coleridge, Sarah Fricker (54) and Sara (22,DNB):The estranged wife and prodigy daughter of Samuel Taylor Coleridge. Although Hartley had moved to Ambleside, Sarah and Sara remained in Keswick with Sarah’s sister, Edith Southey, and her family. In late 1824, the prestigious house of John Murray published Sara’s translation from the French of a book chronicling the Chevalier de Bayard’s exploits. After hosting the Coleridge women at Rydal Mount for a fortnight in April 1825, DW wrote, “Adversity is the best school, I believe, for the best of us; and poor Mrs Coleridge has had enough of it, in the shape of humiliation and disappointed hopes concerning the talents of her Sons. Dear Sara is a sweet creature, so thoughtful and gentle, patient and persevering—and in conversation not disputatious as she used to be.”

(23)
Letters, 4:346.

Cookson, Thomas (54), Elizabeth (50), and 11 children, including Elizabeth (25), James (21), and Edwin (about 8): A large Unitarian family from Staveley, five miles northwest of Kendal. Unrelated to the Cooksons in WW and DW’s maternal line, the “Kendal Cooksons” met the Wordsworths in 1805 through SH, a childhood friend of Elizabeth. The families quickly bonded and thereafter exchanged regular visits, often of a week or more. Prior to his bankruptcy in the late 1820s, Thomas was a successful mill owner and manufacturer of bobbins for textile mills.

(24)
Preliminary and final notices of the Cooksons’ bankruptcy appeared in Aris’s Birmingham Gazette on 4 December 1826, Perry’s Bankrupt Gazette on 21 May 1831, and the Westmorland Gazette on 26 January 1833.
DW was close to the entire family, acting as a surrogate aunt to the 11 Cookson children who had arrived over 20 years: Elizabeth (b. 1799), Strickland (1801), James (1803), John (1804), Thomas (1806), Richard (1808), Henry (1810–1876, DNB), Hannah (1812), Sarah (1814), Edwin (ca. 1816), and Mary (1819).

De Quincey, Thomas (39, DNB) and Margaret Simpson (28): The famed “opium eater” and early WW disciple and his wife, the daughter of a Rydal farmer he had married in 1817 when she was three months’ pregnant. DW had known Margaret as a young child, predicting in 1802 that the then-five-year-old “Little Peggy Simpson” would grow up to look as much like her mother “as any rose in her garden is like that Rose that grew there years before.”

(25)
GJ, 77.
From May 1824 through October 1825, Margaret remained in the Lakes with their growing brood—the child she delivered in late 1824 was their fifth in seven years—while Thomas attempted to stave off creditors by writing feverishly for the London press. During the spring of 1825, in his absence, the family was turned out of Fox Ghyll, their home since 1820, by its new owner, Mrs. Luff. Thomas’s steep fall in the Wordsworths’ eyes is manifest in Dora’s referring to her one-time favorite as “the poor little man” and MW disgustedly reporting in the spring of 1825 that the De Quinceys would soon vacate Fox Ghyll “provided the O. E. [Opium Eater] arrives from London whence he has been expected for weeks & is able to move himself & Family out of it.”
(26)
Letters, 4:388; WT MS WLL / Wordsworth, Mary / 1 / 64.
Various delays ultimately kept Thomas from joining his family at Margaret’s father’s farm until mid-October.

Dowling, Mary (33), Jane (29), and Eliza (28): Unmarried siblings who moved to Ambleside to teach at their sister Ann Dowling Carr’s school and stayed on to run it after her 1824 marriage.

(27)
Letters, 4:240.
Their other accomplished siblings included the judge Sir James Dowling (1787–1844, DNB) and the journalists Vincent George Dowling (1785–1852, DNB) and Alfred Septimus Dowling (1805–1868, DNB). DW befriended the entire family—including their parents, whom she guided about the region in 1823
(28)
Letters 4:203.
—but appears to have been especially close to the youngest, Eliza, in 1825.

Elliott, John (62) and Anna Maria Maltby (53):An apparently childless couple whose two years at Ivy Cottage from late 1822 through December 1824 yielded an enduring friendship with the Wordsworths. He was the eldest son of Rear Adm. George Elliott of Copford, Essex, and she the daughter of a successful Norwich merchant and sister of Edward Maltby (1770–1859, DNB), the reformist clergyman who became Bishop of Chichester in 1830 and Bishop of Durham in 1836.

(29)
John Elliott’s basic life details appear on his tombstone, which was transcribed in The East Anglian 11 (1905–1906):304. Anna Maria Maltby Elliott’s birth, christening, and marriage records are available through Ancestry.com.
In 1832, advance word of their wealth and connections led their housekeeper to fret she would “have some trouble with the new Tenants” and MW to prematurely declare Mrs. Elliott “a fine stately woman” who “expected to find in the Cottage the accommodations of a stately Mansion.”
(30)
LMW, 91.
Such prejudices were quickly dispelled, though, by the Elliotts’ generosity and conviviality. The Wordsworths soon became reliant on both their newspapers and their company, and RJ shows them assisting with the Elliotts’ move to Ambleside and regularly socializing with them thereafter.
(31)
Letters, 3:145–46, 293. After their move, the Elliotts lived in various homes in and around Ambleside, including a stint in 1829–1830 as Mrs. Barlow’s tenants at The Wood.

Harden, John (52), Janet “Jessy” Allan (48), and five children: An Irish gentleman-artist and his socialite wife who married in 1803 and took the lease of Brathay Hall near Ambleside a year later.

(32)
For detailed accounts of John’s art and the family’s life at Brathay, see Foskett, John Harden, and Dybeck, Jessy’s Journal.
John was a respected painter of watercolor landscapes who numbered Constable among his artist friends, and Jessy the daughter of Robert Allan, a prominent Edinburgh banker and proprietor of the Caledonian Mercury. The five Harden children—Robert Allan (1803–1875), Joseph Webster (1805–1872), Jane Sophia (1807–1876), John (1809–1875), and Jessie (1814–1908)—were close friends of the Wordsworth children, which resulted in the adults of the two households socializing more regularly during the 1820s. Three years before Jessy’s 1837 death, they were forced to move to Hawkshead after the home they had leased for three decades was sold to a new owner-occupant.
(33)
Foskett, John Harden, 43, 47–48; MW, 13.

Harrison, Dorothy Wordsworth (23) and Benson (38): WW and DW’s second cousin and the Ulverston widower and iron magnate she married in September 1823.

(34)
Benson Harrison’s father, Matthew (1753–1824), had risen from steward to the owner of the Newland Ironworks to, as of 1812, the foundry’s owner (GJ, 170n). Sir Bernard Burke later identified Benson Harrison as “the head of the oldest iron works establishment in England and Scotland” (Genealogical and Heraldic Dictionary, 1:745).
In her early teens, this younger Dorothy spent a year at Rydal Mount, where she took school lessons from SH and DW, served as an older sister figure for Dora, and, to avoid confusion, was known as “Middle Dorothy” or “Middle Dolly.”
(35)
Letters, 5:112n.
By one report, a fleeting glimpse of the angelic “Middle Dorothy” through an Ulverston shop window in the early 1820s “so enchanted” Benson Harrison “as to haunt his memory till he made her his bride.”
(36)
See Rawnsley’s chapter “The Last of the Rydal Dorothys” in A Rambler’s Notebook, esp. 232–34. His claim that Rydal Mount was Middle Dorothy’s” primary home for six full years (1813–1819) is incorrect.
After settling into her new Ulverston home, “The Lund,” Dorothy bore a son and heir in July 1824. A year later, just weeks after the visit to The Lund that DW records in Notebook 1, she delivered twins, only one of whom survived.
(37)
WG, 6 August 1825.

Jackson, Rev. William (32): The son of Thomas Jackson, Grasmere’s long-time rector until his passing in 1821, who now served as incumbent curate of Whitehaven on the Cumbrian coast. After graduating from Cambridge, Jackson briefly tutored John Wordsworth, and the two remained close, as evidenced by John’s spending weeks at a time in Whitehaven. DW wrote that, while his friends and family wanted Jackson to visit Grasmere more frequently, he was “too zealous in the performance of his duties to consent to stay longer away.”

(38)
Letters, 4:340.
In 1829 he would marry another child of Grasmere, Julia Crump.

Jewsbury, Maria Jane (24, DNB): A new friend of the Wordsworths who since 1821 had been the de facto “house poetess” of her hometown newspaper, the Manchester Gazette. In late 1825, DW described Jewsbury as “a young woman of extraordinary talents, . . . a good Daughter, a good Sister to a numerous Family at the head of which she was left, by the Death of her Mother, at the age of 15.”

(39)
Letters, 4:405.
She had first come to the Wordsworths’ attention earlier that year when she dedicated her debut book, Phantasmagoria, to WW, and she soon forged epistolary friendships with WW, DW, and Dora. Her status as a family favorite was cemented during the summer of 1825, when she visited Rydal Mount, hosted several Wordsworths in Manchester, and vacationed with them at Kents Bank.
(40)
See Letters, 4:342, and Dora’s extensive correspondence with Jewsbury in LDW.
Notebook 2 of the RJ details a week DW spent at the Jewsburys’ Manchester home in February 1826.

Knott, Susannah (51): An infirm but amiable spinster of Green Bank in Ambleside who had lived with her widely beloved grandmother, Dorothy Knott, until the latter died at age 90 in 1820. The Knott family’s roots in the area dated to Elizabethan times, and WW based his “Grasmere Knight” in The Excursion on one of their ancestors.

(41)
In 1815 DW noted that “William and Mary and little Willy paid a visit to old Mrs Knott yesterday with the Excursion in hand, William intending to read to the old Lady the history of the Grasmere Knight” (Letters, 3:202–03).
DW appears to have met Miss Knott through the 1808 charitable campaign for the Green orphans. Susannah would die in July 1828.
(42)
On 19 July 1828, the Carlisle Patriot reported the 7 July death “at Ambleside, [of] Miss Knott, aged 54, after a long illness.”

Luff, Letitia Morris (49): A childless widow who met the Wordsworths in 1801 and had since become one of DW’s closest friends.

(43)
See the GJ entry for 15 October 1801, the first of the Luffs’ many appearances in the journal (35, 198n).
Her belated husband, Capt. Charles Luff, had died in 1815 in Mauritius, where they had lived since leaving Westmorland in 1812. Notebook 1 details Mrs. Luff’s purchasing Fox Ghyll in late 1824 and promptly “ousting” the De Quinceys so she could commence a thorough renovation. Regarding her trademark extravagance, MW indelicately quipped in May 1825, “Mrs Luff is at her wits end and will soon be at the bottom of her purse. She has involved herself with so much work at Fox Ghyll—and she is purchasing furniture as if she was a Jewess.”
(44)
Letters, 4:356.

Marshall, John (59, DNB), Jane Pollard (53), and 11 children: DW’s closest childhood friend and her husband and children, who in 1824 had purchased Hallsteads, the Ullswater estate 16 miles north of Rydal they had leased since the early 1810s.

(45)
GJ, 183n.
John’s fortune (estimated at £2M upon his death in 1845) came through a flax-spinning manufactory in Leeds. After retiring to the Lakes, he served as high sheriff of Cumberland from 1821 to 1826 and Whig MP for Yorkshire from 1826 to 1830. Besides her enduring bond with Jane, DW was also particularly close to her daughters Mary (b. 1799), Cordelia (1803), Jane (1804), and Ellen (1807).

Myers, Julia (13): The recently orphaned daughter of WW and DW’s profligate cousin John Myers.

(46)
Letters, 4:2–3.
After her father’s death, Julia boarded at the Dowlings’ school in Ambleside and divided her holidays and vacations between the homes of the Wordsworths and her other nearby relatives, the Robinsons.

North, Ford (60) and Sarah Ashworth (60): A Liverpool mill owner and his wife who in 1813 sold Rydal Mount (which they had owned since 1802) to the Flemings of Rydal Hall, who then leased it to the Wordsworths. After leaving Rydal, the family settled in Ambleside, where Mr. North farmed land connected to Brathay Hall and worked as an estate agent and magistrate.

(47)
Foskett, John Harden, 23.
In 1823 WW told Lord Lonsdale that he believed Mr. North would “prove a useful magistrate” based on his recent success in pursuing and “apprehend[ing] a worthless Fellow who had robbed Cornstack.”
(48)
Letters, 4:235
Any good will DW felt toward the Norths during the mid-1820s would have been of recent vintage, as she had long resented Mrs. North’s officiousness during the campaign to aid the Greens in 1808, her husband’s “topping and lopping” of an old wood in 1809, and the plodding pace of their 1813 move from Rydal Mount despite the Wordsworths’ anxiousness to move in.
(49)
On 10 May 1808, DW grumbled about how “a Mrs. North, a Liverpool Lady has imported the fashion of [women’s hospitals] into this neighbourhood where, God be thanked, such a thing was never before heard, being not needed” (Letters, 2:232). Almost exactly a year later, she described Mr. North as “a Man hated by all his Neighbours” (2:339). And on 6 April 1813, she recounted being briefly “vexed with [herself] for having spoken so ill-naturedly of the Norths” until their dilatory departure from Rydal Mount quashed any urge to “repent [her] of [her] malice” (3:89–90).

Robinson, Capt. Charles (36) and Charlotte Kearsley (26):Distant cousins of the Wordsworths (on Mr. Robinson’s side) who in 1822, following Mr. Robinson’s retirement from the navy, married and settled in Ambleside. He was the eldest of Adm. Hugh and Mary Myers Robinson’s 13 children, and she hailed from Manchester. After the Robinsons welcomed their firstborn in 1823, DW tartly confided that she wished the infant’s parents “were not themselves quite so much of Babies.”

(50)
Letters 4:218.
Charlotte delivered twins less than 18 months later (as recorded in Notebook 1) and eventually bore 11 children total between 1823 and 1838.
(51)
Regional newspapers subsequently announced “the Lady of Charles Robinson, Esq.” giving birth to a son on 19 February 1826 and daughters on 12 September 1827, 15 June 1829, 25 October 1830, 29 April 1833, and 22 August 1838.

Robinson, Lt. Thomas (30):The dashing young naval officer and distant cousin of the Wordsworths who often spent his shore leave in Ambleside with his older brother Charles. While visiting in the summer of 1823, he apparently took romantic interest in the 19-year-old Dora, as DW quipped at the time that this “pleasant young man” was wont to play her niece’s “protector on her walks.”

(52)
Letters, 4:218.
That he only increased his attentions upon returning in the autumn of 1825 is suggested by various entries in the RJ and Dora’s confiding to a friend, “Recur[ring] to the old subject . . . keeps me in fear & trembling & I have made up my mind to tell my Aunt if he says anything more to me & leave to her the agreeable task of answering for me.”
(53)
LDW, 26.
Whether her affections grew in the months ahead is unclear, but his hopes were quashed for good in early 1826, when WW bluntly told Tom that neither he nor Dora had “sufficient fortune” to make their union feasible.
(54)
Letters, 4:424.
He would never marry, dying in 1838 at age 44.

Scambler, Alice Bownas (43): The widow of Richard Scambler, the Ambleside apothecary and Wordsworth family physician between 1808 and his unexpected death in 1820, an event that left Mrs. Scambler with nine children and few resources. DW had been particularly fond of Mr. Scambler, defending him against charges he might have done more to save the Wordsworths’ infant daughter, Catherine, in 1812. “If you had seen Mr Scambler,” DW told MW and WW, “if you had seen the humility, the tenderness, the watchfulness with which he administered the medicines – if you had heard him speak with the calm confidence and satisfaction that we did[,] you would have been satisfied.”

(55)
Letters, 8:139.
Mrs. Scambler remained in their Ambleside home, Walton Cottage, in 1825, and DW’s visits were likely more charitable than social. Thirteen years later, in 1838, MW reported that Mrs. Scambler, her daughter Elizabeth, “and all the rest of them, poor things, including a grandchild, are going to leave Ambleside, bag and baggage.”
(56)
LMW, 203. For birth, marriage, and death records for Richard and Alice Scambler, see Ancestry.com.

Southey, Robert (50, DNB), Edith (50), Bertha (15), and Kate (14): The family of Britain’s poet laureate, who since 1804 had shared Greta Hall in Keswick with the Coleridges. The Wordsworths and Southeys had grown increasingly close over the preceding decade, and DW’s journal shows the children and adults from both families frequently visiting one another for days, and sometimes weeks, at a time. WW reported in 1824 that his fellow poet “preserves excellent health, and, except that his hair is gryzzled, a juvenile appearance, with more of youthful spirits than most men.”

(57)
Letters, 4:259.
While Edith’s physical and mental health would severely decline over the decade to come, in 1825 she remained relatively active and social. Of the Southeys’ five surviving children—Edith (1804–1871), Bertha (1809–1877), Kate (1810–1864), Isabel (1812–1826), and Cuthbert (1819–1888)—only Bertha and Kate appear in Notebook 1. In a November 1825 letter, DW called them “delightful girls” and Bertha “an especial favorite of [hers].”
(58)
Letters, 4:395.

Tillbrooke, Samuel (42): A bachelor and fellow of Peterhouse College, Cambridge, who owned Ivy Cottage from 1817 to 1831. After meeting him in 1809 through their mutual friends, the Clarksons, DW reported, “We liked Mr Tillbrook very much for his unaffected manners—though there is a coarseness, a want of polish about him which might be smoothed away with advantage.”

(59)
Letters, 2:368–69.
Since, after acquiring Ivy Cottage, he generally leased it out, he often stayed with the Wordsworths when in the area prior to his premature death in 1835.
(60)
Letters, 4:76.

Watson, Dorothy Wilson (74) and four unmarried daughters:The widow and daughters of the late Richard Watson, Bishop of Llandaff (d. 1816), who since 1791 had lived at Calgarth Hall on the Windermere shore. Until his death, the bishop was, by De Quincey’s account, “the leading person about the Lakes, as regarded rank and station, amongst those who had any connexion with literature.”

(61)
De Quincey, “Samuel Taylor Coleridge,” 686.
All five Watson sisters—Dorothy (1777–1837), Harriet (1779–1835), Elizabeth (1781–1859), Charlotte (1783–1859), and Mary (d. 1847)—lived into old age without marrying, and in 1825 several still lived at Calgarth with their mother, to whom the estate had been entailed.



Current or Former Servants

The Wordsworths grew close to many of their servants, and the following women—all of whom appear repeatedly in the 1825 journal—appear to have been current or former domestics, or relatives thereof, who remained in DW’s orbit. Several share first or last names with the laboring-class women DW mentions in her 1812 account of how, at the impromptu funeral for the infant Catherine Wordsworth, “Fanny and Sarah followed [them] . . ., and the Body was borne by Sarah’s Mother, Fanny’s, Mary Williamson’s and Mary Dawson’s.”

(62)
Letters, 8:126.
De Quincey suggested that Westmorland culture was unusual in that, to prepare young women to eventually manage homes of their own, wealthy Dalesmen did “not think it degrading to permit even [their] eldest daughter to go out a few years to service.”
(63)
De Quincey, “Sketches of Life and Manners,” pt. 2, 35.
The Wordsworths’ servants, however, seem to have consistently come from humbler stock, as evidenced by DW invariably using their first names when referencing them in her letters and journals rather than the more formal modes of address (“Mrs. Cookson,” “Miss Barlow”) she used for even close friends from genteel families.

Key Places in Notebook 1

Ambleside (pop. 1,095

(72)
All population figures are from the 1831 census tables republished by the Cumbria History Trust.
; 2 miles southeast of Rydal): Central Lakes market town where the Wordsworths received their mail, socialized, and did much of their shopping.

Brathay Hall (3 miles south of Rydal in Ambleside): Estate on the north shore of Windermere that was the Hardens’ home from 1804 to 1834.

Calgarth Hall (5 miles south of Rydal): Estate near Troutbeck on the northeast shore of Windermere that since 1791 had been the residence of the Watsons, the family of the Bishop of Llandaff.

Fox Ghyll (1 mile south of Rydal): Cottage along the River Rothay in the hamlet of Loughrigg occupied by the De Quinceys between 1820 and the spring of 1825, when they were turned out by its new owner, Letitia Luff.

Grasmere (pop. 395; 2 miles west of Rydal): Village where the Wordsworths resided from 1799 to 1813 and continued to attend church until Rydal Chapel opened in late 1824.

Ivy Cottage: Home (now the Glen Rothay Hotel) down the hill from Rydal Mount owned by Samuel Tillbrooke from 1817 to 1831 and occupied at various times by such friends of the Wordsworths as the Gees, Elliotts, and Quillinans.

Kendal (pop. 10,015; 15 miles southeast of Rydal): Market town and southern gateway to the Lakes that the Wordsworths frequently visited to shop, conduct business, and stay with the Cooksons.

Low Wood (3 miles south of Rydal): Site on Windermere’s east shore known for its annual regatta and the luxury hotel of which WW said, “No inn in the whole district is so agreeably situated for water views and excursions.”

(73)
Link to appropriate paragraph of our RC ed. of the Guide to the Lakes.

Keswick (pop. 2,159; 15 miles northwest of Rydal): Market town in north-central Westmorland in which the Coleridges and Southeys resided at Greta Hall.

Loughrigg: Loughrigg Fell is the 1,100-foot-high hill that, from Rydal Mount, looms over Rydal Water; Loughrigg also signified the general locale of the homes on and below the fell.

Rydal (pop. 315 [combined with Loughrigg]): Wooded, residential hamlet between Ambleside and Grasmere where DW, WW, and MW resided, at Rydal Mount, from 1813 until their deaths in the 1850s.

Troutbeck (pop. 349; 5 miles southeast of Rydal): Hamlet near Ambleside in which the Barlows resided at The Wood.
