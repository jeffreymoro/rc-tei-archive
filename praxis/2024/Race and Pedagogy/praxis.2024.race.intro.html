In that winter meeting, we reflected on how national events in the previous eighteen months had impacted our classrooms. In the wake of the Black Lives Matter movement and the violence that prompted it, it was clear that the words “freedom” and “abolition,” so often found on the syllabi of classes on Romantic literature, were now resounding in universities with new connotations. Students walking straight from campus protests to our classes encountered anti-slavery texts on the syllabi of our surveys on Romantic literature with a new interest in exploring the relationship between the moment these texts were written and the moment in which we were reading them. In a class on John Newton’s poem “Amazing Grace” that happened to be planned for the week following the Amherst Uprising, for instance, the atmosphere was understandably charged, since President Obama had only recently sung the hymn at Clementa Pinckney’s funeral, after he and eight members of the congregation at Emanuel African Methodist Episcopal Church were murdered by a white supremacist in June 2015. After watching a video of his rendition of the hymn, students debated what different lines in the poem might mean. Several students questioned Obama’s decision to sing the hymn after learning about how Newton, the author of the poem that became the hymn, had profited from the trade of enslaved persons before his conversion to Christianity. During this conversation, students became more and more intrigued by how context conjugates the polyvalence of words. One student insisted on the importance of the hymn to her and to her family in moments where tragedy had touched their lives, and taught the class about the importance of the hymn in the Baptist Church. As the class learned about the way that Obama was building on Martin Luther King’s engagement with the hymn during the Civil Rights movement, the debate about what the history of this poem might do to its meaning in the present, and vice versa, intensified.

Conversations in our classrooms that semester awakened a variety of emotions in students that we had not encountered as professors who had taught this material before. Or, at least, many affective responses to texts were on display in classrooms that semester, in a way that they hadn’t been before. In another class that centered on the sentimental tradition in abolitionist verse, students intuited for themselves Alan Richardson’s argument that “read less as literature than as political discourse or social commentary, the anti-slavery poems of the era still fail to move beyond vapid sentiment, stock description, stereotyped characters and situation.”

II. From roundtable to special issue

Returning to 2016: as we examined events on local campuses with each other, we wondered what colleagues in other colleges and universities had been experiencing during this time of mourning and redress. Mindful of the inherent limitations in our positionalities, we sought to engage the collective wisdom of other colleages in a more formal and generative dialogue that would create different possibilities for the classrom. In dialogue with Kate Singer and Lily Gurton-Wachter, co-organizers of the seminar, we resolved to bring together four scholars of race and literature—Frances Botkin, Kimberly Juanita Brown, Sean Gordon, and Patricia A. Matthew—to create a safe and respectful space in which faculty and graduate student instructors could engage in dialogue with each other about how they were currently teaching anti-slavery texts from the Romantic period, and how we might imagine teaching them in the future. Two of our speakers were local, and two travelled from different East-Coast locations to be with us. The roundtable was transatlantic in scope, with scholars representing Caribbean, American and British literatures. In our invitation, we simply asked our colleagues to “speak about approaches to teaching eighteenth or nineteenth-century anti-slavery literature in the context of the Black Lives Matter Movement.” We asked them to share some moments from their own recent experiences in the classroom, and their subsequent reflections on them. We stressed that the emphasis would be on conversation with the audience, whom we imagined would also find it useful to have a space to share their reflections on classroom experience.

An unusually large audience was in attendance at the pedagogy panel in Spring 2018: it was clear that this subject was of interest to students at both the graduate and undergraduate level, as well as to faculty members who more usually made up the audience of the Global Cultures of the Long Nineteenth Century seminar.

(13)
The panel was originally planned for February 17 2017, but a national strike that day meant that we decided to cancel it in solidarity, meaning that it had to be delayed until all the speakers could reconvene, in 2018. Yet the topic only felt more urgent by that time.
Several different pedagogical approaches were offered, but the panelists all shared a desire to confront the violence contained in these eighteenth- and nineteenth-century texts and the silences of the past and to share epistemologies of social justice, speaking to the present with the future in mind. Though the speakers approached the task of teaching different anti-slavery texts with different methodologies, they each gave us an insight into classrooms where students are frequently invited to think about how to connect the past with the present moment. As our panelists discussed a range of approaches to teaching anti-slavery texts, the question of affect was clearly a central concern, as it had been in the discussion that led to the event. Students often have questions about the violence that these texts present them with, and in turn, teachers themselves had many questions for each other about how to frame that act of presentation. How do we support students through challenging readings, beyond giving content warnings or making the readings optional? How does one manage the affect—the emotions and feelings—that such triggering material is bound to bring into the room? How can we make space for conversation in the classroom about how different students might encounter anti-slavery literatures, because of the varied experiences they bring to these texts?

Several important questions about what it means to teach anti-slavery literature in the context of courses on Romanticism also emerged that day. We recall that Kimberly Juanita Brown played music over her own speech as she discussed the power of African American spirituals to invite students to connect to the “auditory dimensions” of the archive, a concept she also explores in her book, The Repeating Body: Slavery’s Visual Resonance in the Contemporary (140); and indeed, many of our panelists asked us to attune their ears to listen for multilayered channels of communication in anti-slavery texts. A central theme in our discussion was the question of how to bring to light the things that the texts do not say about the realities of slavery in the period, either because of the blindspots of the authors, or because of the constraints placed upon them. As Saidiya Hartman asks, where the archive is lacking, how can we “retrieve what remains dormant…without committing further acts of violence” in our own acts of narration (2)?

The essays that follow record various answers to these collective questions. Three of the original speakers at the panel (Frances Botkin, Sean Gordon and Patricia A. Matthew) have contributed essays to this collection. Although Kimberly Juanita Brown was not able to engage in this iteration of the conversation, the impact of her contributions to the panel in a talk on nonetheless remain. We are also pleased that the final collection contains essays by participants who were at different stages in their university careers at the time of the event: Sean Gordon was a graduate student at UMass Amherst at the time of his original contribution to the panel, and Nick Blaisdell, now an high-school teacher, adjunct professor, and activist, was an undergraduate when he attended our seminar. As the project continued, we also decided to gesture towards the shared frame of reference in the room that the Global Cultures seminar had produced, by inviting other participants to join the conversation about pedagogy via the medium of print. Ivan Ortiz and Elizabeth Fay, who had spoken in our series, graciously accepted our invitation. We are also grateful to Debbie Lee and Alan Richardson for agreeing to offer responses to the collection. Eighteen years after they worked together on the pioneering anthology Early Black British Writing (2003), Lee and Richardson not only offer a retrospective view of the shared insights of this collection, but also of the changes that have occurred in the field since 2003.

The genre of writing we asked contributors to engage in as their arguments took shape—something between argumentative prose, critical history, personal narrative, and a practical guide—is one that we are rarely asked to engage in as academics, and we thank the editors of the Romantic Circles Pedagogies for the room they have made within the field for this kind of reflection. Following the work of exemplary scholars and educators such as Paolo Freire, Stuart Hall, bell hooks, and Edward Said, we are humbled by the task of approaching alongside our contributors the question of how our teaching and scholarship can contribute to the advancement of restorative justice in a global community where division and injustice towards those structurally submitted to exclusionary regimes is increasing due to the fallout from Covid 19, economic agendas that put profit over people, climate change, and geopolitical conflict around the globe. As Hall sagely anticipated in his seminal “Teaching Race” (1980): ‘The really crucial question is how do you begin to make that move away from the level of prejudice and belief? One needs to undermine the obvious. One has to show that these are social and historical processes and that they are not written in the stars, they are not handed down. They are deep conditions which are not going to change if we start tinkering around with them. We must not give our students that kind of illusion. We can however begin the process of questioning what the structures are and how they work. (127)’ This collection enters into the spirit of “beginning the process of questioning” with the awareness that there are many more questions to be asked than can be answered in one special issue, and the hopes that new work by colleagues and students will continue to urgently ask them. Because it has arisen out of a call for colleagues within a particular geographically-defined network to describe to us what they were already teaching, rather than from an open call for work about the future of our field at large, we cannot make claims for coverage. We regret that no essays address Romantic anti-slavery literatures from Latin America, Brazil, Africa, Asia, or Australia, or work with Indigenous literatures, translation or multilingualism. We also regret that there is no essay here that addresses in detail the question of how to teach the Haitian Revolution, which is clearly foundational in the study of anti-slavery in the Romantic period. III. Beyond diversifying the canon

Before we begin to describe in detail the interventions that the contributors to this collection make, a brief review of the texts available for teaching the topic of anti-slavery literature, as well as the history of its anthologization, may be useful to those planning to embark on teaching it for the first time, as well as to those wishing to think further about the relationship between how developments in research relate to those in their own pedagogy. Indeed, a commitment to the continued and critical self-examination of genealogies of primary and secondary texts about anti-slavery literature is one that the current moment necessitates. Scholars have long been engaged in the struggle to bring this material into classrooms. Thus, for example, Eva B. Dykes, whose work, as Bakary Diaby has pointed out, should be reasessed, was the earliest critic to call for an expansion of the Romantic canon to include anti-slavery writings (Diaby “Black Women”). Her groundbreaking The Negro in English Romantic Thought: Or a Study of Sympathy for the Oppressed (1942), written in the midst of the Harlem Renaissance and also at the height of Jim Crow segregation in the United States, drew attention to names such as Olaudah Equiano, Ignatius Sancho, Ottobah Cugoano, and Mary Prince, not yet present in any anthologies of Romantic-era literature. The reassessment of Dyke’s work seems particularly necessary when one notes that a forty-year silence passed between her study in 1942 and 1985, when Charles T. Davis and Henry Louis Gates Jr. published their pivotal anthology The Slave’s Narrative (1985), which printed excerpts from Equiano, Cugoano, Sancho and James Alberst Ukawsaw Gronniosaw, among others.

It was not until the 1990s that access to anti-slavery literature became more widespread. Inspired both by the historicist and postcolonial turn in Romantic-era scholarship, scholars in the United States and Britain worked to incorporate Black Atlantic authors into the Anglo-American canon throughout this period.

(15)

Despite the efforts of many scholars to join in the work of recovery and to redress the biases of twentieth-century criticism and editing of Romantic texts, the topic of “slavery and abolition” is still frequently framed as an additionto the anthologies that undergraduate classes rely on, as well as in syllabi themselves.

Digital searches of databases of online syllabi suggest that a handful of texts, especially in surveys of the period, are being asked to carry a heavy freight. Many and varied obstacles have been put in the way of affirming the contributions of Black and Brown writers from the period in the classroom. The tradition that has elided subjects who historically were denied ontological value, and who were silenced through enslavement and racism in the economic and geopolitical system that was Atlantic Slavery, can still be observed today in this disconnect between research and pedagogy.

IV. Contrapuntal Reading

Beyond simply adding texts to established syllabi, then, the contributors in this collection imagine many different ways to center the question of how to disrupt pedagogical traditions that have been handed down from past generations of scholars.These scholars invite readers to examine the degree to which their own pedagogy reflects transformations in the field, even as they push for further re-examination of the structures that pedagogical attitudes to teaching anti-slavery have previously been built on. Our contributors tend to agree that because Romantic era accounts of slavery were conditioned by the period in which they were written, it’s “not enough,” as Frances Botkin puts it, to ask them to speak on behalf of the enslaved without other sources to contextualize them. Dykes poignantly draws attention to this paradox at the center of Romantic anti-slavery literature in the opening pages of her text. “One of the salient features of the ‘Romantic Movement’ of the latter eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries was the love of freedom” (1), she writes, paying tribute to Mary Prince’s famous line, “To be free is very sweet” (27). However, she then immediately notes that Romantic writers could also be “limited… by an adherence to traditional forms and conventions” (1), and notes that anti-slavery writings did not always depose the hierarchical structure and racism that authorized the triangular trade in the first place. Many scholars since Dykes have complicated any simple celebration of the expansion of the Romantic canon to include anti-slavery texts by exploring the capacity of these texts to sometimes “popularize and codify racial prejudice,” despite their ethical and civic commitments to breaking the Blakean “mind forg’d manacles” that sustained Atlantic Slavery (Lee and Richardson 7), and the contributors to this collection extend this project.

It has been intriguing for us to see that the authors in this collection have arrived at a shared methodology regarding how to approach teaching anti-slavery literatures today organically, without a prompt from us and without reading drafts of each others’ essays. Many of them reframe and resituate eighteenth- and nineteenth-century texts in new constellations, not only in relation to each other and to art and artefacts from the period, but also in relation to texts from Black Studies, and to twentieth and twenty-first works of fiction and critical fabulation that seek to address the silences of the past. In their commitment to this method, several contributors implicitly employ contrapuntal reading, a method that since Fernando Ortiz's Contrapunteo cubano del tabaco y el azúcar (1944) and Edward Said’s Culture and Imperialism (1993), who independently of each other advanced the method, has influenced the work of writers like Cuban novelist Alejo Carpentier and critics such as Mary Louise Pratt and Simon Gikandi.

In the Anglo-American Academy, readers may be familiar with how Said theorizes the importance of reading “not univocally but contrapuntally, with an awareness both of the metropolitan history that is narrated and of those other histories against which (and together with which) the dominating discourse acts” (Said 51). Drawing on music to describe a form of attention to reading novels such as Austen’s Mansfield Park, he writes: ‘In the counterpoint of classical music, various themes play off one another, with only a provisional privilege being given to any particular one; yet in the resulting polyphony there is concert and order, an organized interplay that derives from the themes, not from a rigorous melodic or formal principle outside of the work. In the same way, I believe, we can read and interpret English novels, for example, whose engagement (usually suppressed for the most part) with the West Indies or India, say, is shaped and perhaps even determined by the specific history of colonization, resistance, and finally native nationalism. (51)’ While Said's archive was “English novels,” Fernando Ortiz applied “contrapunteo” in his anthropological analysis of sugar and tobacco written fifty years prior to Said’s work, and in which he coined the word “transculturación”— transculturation—by now familiar to critics through the work of Mary Louise Pratt. It should be noted that Ortiz and his generation, which included Lydia Cabrera, the renown scholar of santería, and Alejo Carpentier, who turned to the Haitian Revolution for inspiration, embarked on a reassessment of the contributions of AfroCuban and African cultures to Cuban identity. Ortiz emphasizes the musical roots of counterpoint at the same time that he undercores the AfroCuban experience, one that incorporates both music and the orality of verse in call and response rituals between griot and the audience. “Recordemos en Cuba sus manifestaciones más floridas en las preces antifonarias de las liturgias, así de blancos como de negros, en la controvertida erótica y danzarina de la rumba, y en los contrapunteos versificados de la guajirada montuna y de la currería afrocubana" (F. Ortiz 18). [Let us remember the elaborate manifestations [of counterpoint] in Cuba, whether in the antiphonal liturgies of Blacks and whites, in the controversial erotics of the movement of the rumba, and in the versified counterpoints in country and Afrocuban peoples]. In his novel about the Haitian Revolution, El siglo de las luces (1962), Carpentier develops the aesthetics of the “arbitrario contrapunto” (“the arbitrary counterpoint”) by considering the different, yet simultaneous unfolding of the lives of his protagonists (47), which shape and impact individual spheres of action as well as the grand récit of history. Carpentier later extends in his essay about "lo real maravilloso," the marvellous real, where he again returns to the Haitian Revolution, Henri Cristophe’s opposition to Napoleon, and Mackandal, "que hace creer a millares y millares de esclavos en Haití ... que puede transformarse en lo que quiera, y promueve con ellos una de las primeras revoluciones auténticas del Nuevo Mundo" [Mackandal, who convinces thousands and thousands of slaves in Haiti ... that he can turn into anything, and promotes with them one of the first authentic revolutions of the New World] (Carpentier 132).

Simon Gikandi further develops the contrapuntal method when he applies it to reading the archive of Atlantic slavery and anti-slavery in Slavery and the Culture of Taste (2011).

Gikandi synchronizes the spatial, temporal, and interior dimensions of the lives of subjects as apparently disconnected as diarist Anna Larpent and Nealee, the enslaved woman in Mungo Park’s narrative. In other chapters, he considers texts by Francis Williams, Hume and Olaudah Equiano; Phillis Wheatley, William Beckford, and Thomas Jefferson (among others) in contrapuntal relationship to each other. Gikandi accounts for how “the criteria driving the aesthetic ideology in the eighteenth century—disinterest and virtue, among others—had come to be haunted by slavery” (Gikandi 144). The effect of this more deliberate counterpoint is to ensure “a dialectic between the visible subjects of modernity and its invisible actors” (70), as he writes. Exegesis as an act of resistance exposes the symbolic systems that mediate materiality, disturbing the cognitive and symbolic separations between the violent system of the plantation secured through empire, and the well-wrought tea set of the drawing room, holding actors accountable for their complicity with such violence.

Patricia A. Matthew draws attention to how her students themselves implicitly enacted a contrapuntal method when they responded to the suppressed context of William Blake’s “The Little Black Boy” in a class in 2006. Her essay offers a retrospective account of howthis moment offered an invitationto continue to explore, in several different settings (both undergraduate and graduate),the effects of centering the topic of race and slavery in the classroom. Matthew invites makers of syllabi to look beyond the question of which single texts to use in a self-contained “unit” of a syllabus on slavery, to instead imagine how Romanticism looks whenone centers “race, abolition and freedom” on the level of the syllabus as a whole. The essay criticizes the idea that one can simply add a single text about slavery to a syllabus and expect that the topic has been covered, as if a Romantic literature syllabus is a familiar recipe one can add a new ingredient to. Rather than focusing a discussion about Atlantic slavery on a single text in a syllabus, Matthew argues, it should be considered a relevant context in discussions of all Romantic texts, even in texts where it seems not to be present. Matthew describes two different courses which center anti-slavery literatures and texts about race, including detailed discussions ofteaching Blake’s poem, Hannah More and Eaglesfield Smith’s “The Sorrows of Yamba,” Amelia Opie’s “The Black Man’s Lament; or, How to Make Sugar,” Coleridge’s 1795 lecture “On the Slave Trade,” and the anonymous The Woman of Colour, A Tale, A Novel(1808). Ivan Ortiz’ method can also be called contrapuntal, from several perspectives. Ortiz puts Said’s argument about the silences in Austen’s Mansfield Park (1814) in dialogue with Lynn Festa’s argument that Austen is also asking readers to listen for “noise.” Ortiz begins by explaining how he uses this principle of “listening for noise” as a method to teach abolition poetry written by white authors. He positions this poetry in counterpoint with music, when he asks students to examine the complexity of a situation in which both Hannah More’s version of “The Sorrows of Yamba” and William Cowper’s “The Negro’s Complaint” were sung to the well-known ballad “Admiral Hosier’s Ghost,” a tune about white English sailors who had died at sea. He then turns to a text that could be called contrapuntal in itself; M. NourbeSe Philip’s Zong! (2008), which is “composed entirely from the words of the case report, Gregson vs. Gilbert, related to the murder of Africans on board a slave ship at the end of the eighteenth century.”

As students center their attention on the silences that this text exposes, they are confronted with the question of “what the fragmentation of language accomplishes that a ballad narrative cannot.”

Sean Gordon also extends the contrapuntal method beyond the frame of synchronicity, to ask how twentieth-century narratives might also inform students’ understandings of nineteenth-century testimonies. He describes the project of “teaching contemporary texts that take up Romantic-era concepts in a speculative framework,” like Celu Amberstone’s “Refugees” (2004), Octavia Butler’s Bloodchild and Other Stories (1995/2005), and Samuel Delany’s Empire Star (1966), in relation to Olaudah Equiano’s Interesting Narrative. To frame his discussion of what it meant to discuss these texts on a campus responding to a series of violently racist incidents in 2014, Gordon centers Fred Moten’s term “dehiscence” (which describes a form of structural vulnerability that allows for productive disordering), as well as the Afropessimism of Frank Wilderson, pointing toward another speculative future, in which a conversation between scholars of Black Studies and scholars of Romanticism will become more common. He also discusses how teaching Mat Johnson’s Pym (2011) in dialogue with Edgar Allan Poe’s Narrative of Arthur Gordon Pym (1838) allowed students to think through the issue of how structures of white privilege are implicit not only in the archive, but in the university itself.

Afrofuturism provides a site of conceptual resistance in several of the essays here. Leading sociologist Alondra Nelson defines Afrofuturism as a mode which uplifts “‘African American voices’ with ‘other stories to tell about culture, technology and things to come,’” and often concerns “sci-fi imagery, futurist themes, and technological innovation in the African diaspora” (9). Through her syllabus and classroom discussion, Elizabeth Fay stages an innovative conversation between Afrofuturism, Romanticism, and Atlantic Studies that juxtaposes past and present narratives of the legacies of enslavement, and challenges students to reinterpret Atlantic history by engaging “future history imagined” (13). When Fay asks students to compare Olaudah Equiano’s narrative with the film Black Panther (2018), and contextualizes this question in relation to N.K. Jemisin’s introduction Afrofuturism in “How Long ’til Black Future Month?,” she extends the possibilities for the interpretation of “geographical space, conceptual space, and textual space” (Dillon 31) in Black Atlantic narratives.

In the broader sweep of her article, Fay emphasizes “the fluid movement of dynamic relationality among multiple, and variable subjects” as she explores various exercises designed to ask students to triangulate narratives of enslavement and post-enslavement. For instance, Fay asks students to consider how Mary Prince’s nineteenth-century rhetorics of suffering and resistance compare with those of Equiano, and then further complicates the question by asking them to read the rhetoric of Toussaint L’Ouverture’s letters in relation to C.L.R. James’ account of his life in Black Jacobins. (Sylvia Wynter’s article on C.L.R. James, “Beyond the Categories of the Master Conception,” also offers students an argument about how James’ work helps to posit a theory of “becoming Black” which is relevant “whether the year is 1800 or 2021.”) In another unit of the syllabus, Fay triangulates Lenora Sansay’s Secret History; or the Horrors of St. Domingo (1808) and the anonymous The Woman of Colour (1808) with Austen’s Mansfield Park (1814), as she asks students to compare fictionalized accounts of women’s experience in the period, and further contextualizes this exercise with references to the work of Christina Sharpe and Sylvia Wynter.

Frances Botkin also offers many possibilities for students to read contrapuntally when she insists that Romantic-era narratives cannot be taught without a serious commitment to contextualization. Through a theorization of the practice of tunyuhan, Botkin uses what she terms an “undisciplined mode of assembly” to consider the value of generative improvisation in teaching, and how contemporary art, film, music and recordings of interviews with people in Jamaica “animate colonial textuality with the creative expressions of Afro-Caribbean culture that in revising the past, imagines and nourishes future worlds.” She describes, for instance, how she presents students with Lydia Maria Child’s Narrative of Joanna: An Emancipated Slave, of Suriname (1834) (a narrative extracted from John Gabriel Stedman’s The Narrative of a Five Years Expedition against the Revolted Negroes of Surinam [1796]), alongside John Earle’s Obi; or the History of Three-Fingered Jack (1800), a painting of Three-Fingered Jack by the Jamaican painter Omari “Afrikan” Ra, and a documentary film by Roy Anderson called Queen Nanny: Legendary Maroon Chieftainess (2015); a constellation which allows students to see how “Child almost entirely excludes the Maroons with whom Joanna had family connections.” When she asks students to read Mary Prince’s The History of Mary Prince, A West Indian Slave (1831) alongside Marlon James’ The Book of Night Women (2009), she puts further pressure on the question of what nineteenth-century writers do not say about the experiences of enslaved people, and particularly of enslaved women.

Another way in which the dialogic space can be transformed to create an anti-racist classroom is through the acknowledgment of the tension between writing and speech, foregrounding for students the incomplete nature of the anti-slavery archive. In her focus on “doing the work” of travelling to Jamaica in order to better understand Maroon history, Botkin brings centers the notion of orality in order to problematize the hierarchy of knowledge that privileges the archive. Her field work and connection to Maroon leader Gaa’ma Gloria Simms has enabled her to create a contact zone in the classroom where the oral histories of Afro-Caribbean descendants of Maroon communities expose asymmetries of power and the injustices encoded as silence in the written record. The conference in Jamaica which she co-organizes with Paul Youngquist reconfigure the spaces and possibilities of knowledge for Romanticists which brings the “archive” into conversation with living indigenous knowledge.

Nicholas Blaisdell elucidates how countepoint as exegesis awakens agency, as Carpentier also discovered in El siglo de las luces, when Sofía, one of his protagonists, urges, "¡Hay que hacer algo!" [Something must be done] (410). Blaisdell explores how anti-slavery writers like Cugoano indicte capitalism's use of enslaved labor in the 19th century as a global system, anticipating the system's accomodation of unfree labor through the present era. Drawing on Freire, he reflects on how study of anti-slavery texts, abolitionist networks, and Marx informed his development as a social justice activist and organizer of Black Lives Matter Protests in Peabody, Massachusetts, his work as a high school teacher and Adjunct Professor of education. His analysis illuminates how a Freirean pedagogy can translate into greater empowerment for students in educational and civic settings.

V. Some Future Directions

The project of articulating linkages that connect the teaching praxis of Romanticists with histories of resistance, liberation, struggles against slavery, and the oppression of people of color, goes far beyond the purview of a single special issue, and so we wish to close by noting some of the areas for pedagogical inquiry that our contributors have opened vistas onto. We hope that the pedagogical experiments detailed in this collection will be useful in practical terms, as a guide for those teaching in universities in how to begin to redesign their own syllabi and classroom discussions. The larger aim, however, is for the collection to be useful in broader methodological terms, as a prompt for readers to imagine their own contrapuntal arrangements of texts and voices. The possibilities for new contrapuntal readings and new constellations of texts go far beyond the specific texts or arrangements listed in these case studies.

Many of our contributors use specific examples to describe a method that could be applied to various texts. The example Patricia A. Matthew gives, for example, of what it means to read Blake’s work in dialogue with anti-slavery literatures, encourages pedagogues to look more closely at how texts they have taught without reference to the history of slavery might change when this context is brought to bear. Similarly, Sean Gordon and Elizabeth Fay’s references to Afrofuturist texts invite readers to conduct their own research in this field, and to imagine their own conversations between the past and speculative futures. Another question that emerges from this focus on Afrofuturism is whether there might be room for more opportunites for students to encounter examples of Black resistance, humour, joy, and agency in the Romantic classroom. New possibilities for dialogue with contemporary texts will continue to arise as new works of critical fabulation appear (even during the time we have been editing this collection, for instance, many of our colleagues have begun to teach Honorée Fanonne Jeffers’ The Age of Phillis [2020,]).

In an era when zoom makes possible new kinds of collaboration, geographical distance has become less of an obstacle to conversation since we began working on these questions, but the field of Romanticism, and especially studies of slavery in the Romantic period, still needs to do more to address the divisions it has historically instantiated between Anglophone texts and texts in other languages. The Anglophone focus of the authors examined throughout this collection points to multilingualism and Romanticism as a related area for future research as the plurilingual nature of the histories of African and indigenous resistance comes further into view. As Gillian Sankoff writes, ‘It is difficult to conceive of another situation where people arrived with such a variety of native languages, where they were so cut off from their native language groups; where the size of no language group was enough to ensure its survival; where no second language was shared by enough people to serve as a useful vehicle of intercommunication; and where the legitimate [=official-RM] language was inaccessible to almost everyone. (qtd. in Mesthrie 230)’ For some of the writers in the archive of anti-slavery literatures, the process of translation into English and language acquisition was also a process of living through epistemic violence. Yet the governing assumption in Romantic era studies in approaching the anti-slavery archive is that the language of Black British authors is English (Almeida). A similar assumption governs the Indigenous archive, as Nikki Hessell shows in Romantic Literature and the Colonised World: Lessons from Indigenous Translations (2018). Hessell argues that Indigneous interpretations of canonical authors are more than “novelties, nor simple evidence of the global power and reach of Romantic literature” (229), but “simultaneous expressions of the value of Romantic literature and of the experience of colonization” (230). While some might respond that as scholars of British or American literature the field is Anglocentric by nature, this commitment to monolingualism out of habit robs the field of depth and breadth, since the African diaspora was not limited to English-speaking countries. During and even after the abolition of slavery in 1833, Britain continued to trade with the Global South, as scholars such as Rebecca Cole Heinowitz, Omar Miranda, César Soto, Juan Sánchez, Jesse Reeder, and Sara Medina Calzada show, while at the same time making greater imperial incursions in Africa and India. Even so, texts from this “second slavery,” as Dale Tomich has called it, have a belated entrance into Romanticism, such as Juan Manzano’s The Life and Poems of a Cuban Slave (1840), translated by Richard Robert Madden, who also testified a trial that vindicated the rights of enslaved persons to rise up against their enslavers, and was one of the most international trials of its time: The Amistad. The biography of the enslaved Brazilian, Mahommah Gardo Baquaqua (1848) is another useful starting point for those wishing to expand their students’ reference points beyond texts written in English (see Aljoe, “Testimonies” 111). Nicole Aljoe also challenges “the marginal status of West Indian slave narratives within critical discourses of the slave narrative genre, Caribbean and Black Atlantic literary histories,” and reveals how Abolitionist groups in England, the United States, and the West Indies were in close contact with one another.

Concluding Openly (or An Open Conclusion)

The essays in this collection explore how recent history informs the way that students might currently receive Romantic texts, clearing a space for further discussion about how the conversation will continue to change the way that the Romantic texts signify. As scholars and teachers, becoming conscious of not only how pedagogical praxis might be shaped by developments in research and critical race theory, but also how events in the classroom might reshape research is more than a matter of good teaching or meeting the publish or perish expectations of the academy. Taken together, these essays encourage scholars to reflect on how both instructor and students must consider the relation between the classroom and the university, and its relationship to the imagined community at the national and planetary levels. As Matthew, Gordon, Richardson, and Lee elucidate, instructors and students have to grapple with how structures of white privilege are implicit in the university, the archive, and in the discipline so that together they can transform interpretation into a dialogic act of resistance as they deconstruct racialist and exclusionary discourses, thereby creating alternative spaces of true interrelational knowledge together.

The co-creation of knowledge is one of the paths towards the the liberatory potential of education that Paulo Freire, a major inspiration for the 2018 panel, describes. Arguing against the “banking concept” of education, a system in which students merely bank information they passively receive from their teacher, Freire challenges the asymmetry of power central to the subject/object dichotomy that is also at the core of the ontological negation racism relies on. As bell hooks writes in her meditation on how Freire’s pedagogy informs her own, “Freire’s work affirmed that education can only be liberatory when everyone claims knowledge as a field in which we all labor” (Teaching to Transgress 14). Moments of first encounter are often formative: students are coming to texts about slavery from a different historical perspective than their teachers, and this means that students have the potential to teach their teachers things they have not before seen, and which they may find hard to see.

This conversation offers some specific practices in the attempt to create classroom environments to create a place of “where both [professors and students] are simultaneously teachers and students” while respecting the intersectionalities of race, gender, and class in our increasingly diverse student body (Freire 72), but it is by no means exhaustive. bell hooks also cautions that “teachers are often among that group most reluctant to acknowledge the extent to which white-supremacist thinking informs every aspect of our culture including the way we learn, the content of what we learn, and the manner in which we are taught” (Teaching Community 25). With this in mind, the discussions here respectfully offer multiple ways to reflect on how we might more consciously acknowledge and evaluate how our own experiences inflect our pedagogical practices, and how to work with students to create spaces in our classrooms for this vital dialogue. At a time when events in the US have inspired civic action and debate in countries across the world, the present moment can no longer be kept outside the doors of a classroom in which Romantic literature is being read. As scholars, it is useful to consider not only how pedagogical praxis might be reshaped by developments in research, but also how events in the classroom might reshape research practices.
