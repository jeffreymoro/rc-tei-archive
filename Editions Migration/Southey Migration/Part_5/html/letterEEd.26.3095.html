3095. Robert Southey to Grosvenor Charles Bedford, 17 March 1818

Endorsement: 17 March 1818
MS: Bodleian Library, MS Eng. Lett. d. 47. ALS; 2p.
Unpublished.

My dear G.

Gifford will deliver you the proofs of an Article on the Poor Laws,

(1)
Southey’s article (co-written with Rickman), ‘On the Poor Laws’, was published in Quarterly Review, 18 (January 1818), 259–308.
which, as you will perceive is a paper of no little importance, – & for obvious reasons, the less my name is connected with it the better, – one reason why I thought proper to praise the Ed: Review there.
(2)
Edinburgh Review, Southey’s main enemy and a journal to which Thomas Robert Malthus (1766–1834; DNB) was a frequent contributor. Southey detested Malthus’s arguments about the poor, as he made clear inQuarterly Review, 8 (December 1812), 319–356. This section on the Edinburgh Review was not printed.
Now the reason why the proofs are to be handed to you is, that (without letting it be known to any person) you may give them to Rickman who will check the article against all the Parl: Documents referred to therein & return it to you, for Gifford. – But do not mention that it has been in R’s hands.
(3)
This secrecy was probably because Rickman was a civil servant in the office of The Speaker of the House of Commons and did not want his name to be associated with a controversial article.

I am weary of expecting this box, which seems to be destined to be delayed by every imaginable combination of circumstances. It is nine months till I since it set off from Milan, & it has not got to Keswick yet!

(4)
A consignment of books Southey had bought in Milan in June 1817.

When you have any assets in your hands, & happen to be near Temple Bar, send me I pray you from Twinings 24 pounds of Souchong Tea (common tea; between six & seven shillings a pound) & three pounds of green, at 10 or 12 shillings

(5)
Twinings tea merchants were located in the Strand, London, near Temple Bar.

God bless you
RS.

Keswick 17 March.

Oh! – I had nearly forgotten to say that there is a dreadful report in town concerning Greenwich Hospital, – all their woods it is said are to be cut down. My dear G. think of Castlelet! Is there nobody who can point out to the persons concerned that thinning these woods instead of cutting dow all down, would be as much more advantageous for the estate, – as it would be desirable for the country wherein they stand. Especially in Castlelet – where there are trees which fifty years hence will be fine timber.

(6)
Castelet is a wooded hill near Derwentwater, once part of the estates of James Radclyffe, 3rd Earl of Derwentwater (1689–1716; DNB), which were forfeited to the Crown after his execution for treason and were administered by the commissioners of Greenwich Hospital for disabled sailors (founded 1692). In 1817 concern about the estate’s lack of profitability had led to a review of its operations. Southey was clearly affected by local gossip because at this time there were no plans to cut down the trees he mentions here.
