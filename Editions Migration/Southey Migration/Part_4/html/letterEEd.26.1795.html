1795. Robert Southey to Charles Watkin Williams Wynn, [shortly after 28 July 1810]

Address: To/ C W Williams Wynn Esqr. M.P./ Wynnstay/ Wrexham
MS: National Library of Wales, MS 4813D
Unpublished.
Dating note: Lord and Lady Holland visited Southey in Keswick on Saturday 28 July 1810, this letter was written shortly afterwards. See also, Robert Southey to John Rickman, 1 August 1810, Letter 1801.

My dear Wynn

Will you frank the inclosed (because it contains an inclosure) to John May Esqr – Richmond – Surry.

Ld & Lady Holland were here on Saturday. I dined with them & they drank tea with me. The Lady was uncommonly civil, & they nearly extorted a promise that when I went to their Library, Holland House should be my home. I am however very desirous to avoid this, for I have so much the freedom & the feeling of home at Rickmans, that it would be very unpleasant to me to be any where else, even for a few days. – He is translating the Spanish play of the Cid

(1)
Probably Guillén de Castro (1569–1631), Las Mocedades del Cid (1605–1615). Holland’s translation was not published.
– which may be of some use to the sale of my book.
(2)
The Chronicle of the Cid (1808).
He has just such the very books which I want – the modern Spanish publications which are too costly to be within my reach. But I conceive my own collection to be more curious than his, & far richer in old books.

Allen

(3)
John Allen (1771–1843; DNB), political and historical writer, especially on Spanish topics in the Edinburgh Review. He lived at Holland House and often travelled with Lord and Lady Holland.
was loud in reprobating our treatment of Denmark – God help this poor country! – always contriving to do the worst things possible in the worst possible way. –
(4)
Probably a reference Britain’s attack on the Danish fleet in 1801 and bombardment of Copenhagen in 1807.

How is Bedford – & talking of Dapples

(5)
A nickname for Bedford.
– how is the bruise?

God bless you
RS.
