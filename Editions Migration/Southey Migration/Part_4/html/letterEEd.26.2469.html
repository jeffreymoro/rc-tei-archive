2469. Robert Southey to Grosvenor Charles Bedford, 19 August 1814

Address: To/ G. C. Bedford Esqre
Endorsement: 19. Augt. 1814
MS: Bodleian Library, MS Eng. Lett. c. 25
Unpublished.

Keswick 19 Aug. 1814
My dear G.

Still you misunderstand either my meaning, or the nature of blank verse, & conceive cadences & contractions to be irregularities, which are perfectly regular; & employed ad libitum,

(1)
‘at will’.
without being intended to produce any particular beauty of effect, by Milton & the Elizabethan Dramatists, – as any page of any one of them will abundantly prove.
(2)
Southey here responds to Bedford’s critique of MS versions of Roderick, the Last of the Goths (1814).

I have added about 30 lines at the close of the 21st section

(3)
Roderick, the Last of the Goths (1814), Book 21, lines 435–469.
with the best effect. This section having no action of its own (thoughx necessary to prepare & render probable the temper of Julian at his death) could only be made to finish well by what a common place critic would pronounce a bathos, – by passing from passion to repose: & this by the use of description made subservient to moral feeling, is I think well effected

Knox

(4)
John William Knox (1784–1862), clergyman, scholar and usher at Westminster School 1806–1821.
is pickling himself in the sea, but returns to Keswick in a few days. He improves upon acquaintance – & we like him well. – He prays for an English dedication, – & as the only two persons who have seen the intended one agree in this, I suppose they fairly represent the general opinion. So English let it be.
(5)
Roderick, the Last of the Goths (1814) was to be dedicated to Bedford. Southey had vacillated between writing a Latin or English dedication. Although he preferred the former, Bedford, to whom a Latin version had been sent, wished for the latter; see Southey to Grosvenor Charles Bedford, 17 July 1814 (Letter 2461) and 7 August 1814 (Letter 2467).
– I had nearly forgot to thank you for the European
(6)
A ‘Memoir of Robert Southey, Esq.’ and a portrait ‘Engraved by Blood, from an Original Drawing by Edridge [Henry Edridge (1768–1821; DNB)], in the Possession of G.C. Bedford, Esq.’, European Magazine, 66 (July 1814), [3]-5. Southey’s date of entry at Westminster was mistakenly given as 1787.
& your good offices therein. There are two <is one> inaccuraciesy – I did not go to Westminster till 1788. – & my engagement with Corry is represented a little too complaisantly to the Irish Chancellor, whose real tho secret intention was to convert me into a tutor for his son.

God bless you – I have a table covered with proof sheet, & letters to be answered
RS.
