II. Sources of Differences in Meaning when “The cut worm forgives the plow” is Cited Online [sample]

  • “Worm” can either signify an actual worm or serve as a metaphor for a wide range of things, including a human (either an everyman figure or a more specific person, like a soldier, an employee, or a war-torn people), any animal, any entity that lacks the capability of exacting revenge, and a word.
  • The literal meaning of “cut worm” signifies different things to different people, including harmed worms, dead or sacrificed worms, or two live worms.
  • “Forgives” signifies different things to different people, including something like “accepts as true” and “accepts as good.” In the case of the latter, some translate “good” as “beneficial to the greater good” and others as “beneficial to oneself.”
  • “Plow” or the act of plowing becomes a metaphor for a wide range of things, including a psychological challenge to an individual’s sense of self, a sharp tool of agricultural cultivation, anything that causes individual suffering or pain, the activity of subsistence living, the activity of cultural or national transformation/improvement, a powerful force heedless to anything other than itself, the course of history, the course of nature, and workplace cutbacks.
  • Some people cite the proverb as if its wisdom is directed to a potential cut worm, whereas others cite it as if its wisdom is for a potential plow or plow-wielder.
  • Depending on context, the proverb-citer may be asking her hearer to accept (or, alternatively, to lament others’ acceptance of) a plow that has already come through, to encourage an approach to future plowing that could or inevitably will happen, or actually to engage in plowing.
